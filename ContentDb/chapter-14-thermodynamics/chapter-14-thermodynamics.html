Chapter Snapshot - Thermodynamics

One of the most formula-dense and concept-rich chapters in NEET physics, thermodynamics tests the interplay of heat, internal energy, and work through precise sign conventions and process-specific equations. The First Law (ΔQ = ΔU + ΔW), four thermodynamic processes (isothermal, adiabatic, isobaric, isochoric), the Carnot efficiency formula η = 1 − T₂/T₁, and COP of refrigerator are the four pillars of NEET scoring here. Mistakes cluster around one axis: sign convention confusion (work done BY vs ON the system, heat absorbed vs released, ΔU positive vs negative in adiabatic compression). Students who memorise the process table and apply absolute-temperature Kelvin in Carnot will harvest 3–4 marks reliably.

1) First Law of Thermodynamics and Processes

States and applies the First Law ΔQ = ΔU + ΔW (conservation of energy in thermodynamic systems) to all four standard processes: isobaric (P constant, W=PΔV=μRΔT, Q=μCpΔT), isochoric (V constant, W=0, Q=ΔU=μCvΔT), along with sign conventions for heat, work, and internal energy. Internal energy of ideal gas ΔU = μCvΔT for any process. Work done by gas W = ∫P dV = area under PV curve.

ΔQ = ΔU + ΔWIsobaric: W=PΔV=μRΔTIsochoric: W=0, Q=ΔUSign: expansion W>0, absorption Q>0
›
First Law Statement and Sign ConventionsFirst Law: ΔQ = ΔU + ΔW — heat supplied to a system equals increase in internal energy plus work done by system. Sign conventions: ΔQ positive when heat absorbed by system; ΔW positive when gas expands (work done BY system); ΔU positive when temperature rises. Internal energy: ΔU = μCvΔT = μ(R/(γ−1))ΔT = (PfVf − PiVi)/(γ−1) — valid for any process of an ideal gas. Work done W = ∫P dV; area between PV curve and volume axis; positive for expansion, negative for compression. In PV cyclic process: clockwise cycle → positive work; anticlockwise → negative work; net area = net work done.
›
Isobaric and Isochoric Process EquationsIsobaric process (P=constant): Charle's law obeyed V/T=constant. W = P(Vf−Vi) = μRΔT. Q = μCpΔT where Cp = (f/2+1)R. ΔU = μCvΔT. Bulk modulus K=0 (pressure does not change). Isobaric expansion: ΔU, ΔW, ΔQ all positive. Isobaric compression: all negative. Examples: boiling of water and freezing at constant pressure. Isochoric process (V=constant): Gay-Lussac's law P/T=constant. W = 0 since ΔV=0. Q = ΔU = μCvΔT = μR/(γ−1)ΔT. Cv = (f/2)R. Bulk modulus K=∞. Isochoric heating: pressure increases, ΔQ and ΔU positive. Slope on PV diagram is infinite (vertical line).

2) Isothermal and Adiabatic Processes

Isothermal process: T=constant, ΔU=0, Q=W=μRT ln(Vf/Vi); Boyle's law PV=constant; PV graph is rectangular hyperbola. Adiabatic process: Q=0, ΔU=−W; Poisson's law PVγ=constant, TVγ−1=constant; slope of adiabatic curve = γ × slope of isothermal curve. Work in adiabatic: W = μR(Ti−Tf)/(γ−1). Adiabatic expansion cools, adiabatic compression heats. Free expansion: Q=0, W=0, ΔU=0.

Isothermal: ΔU=0, Q=W=μRT ln(V₂/V₁)Adiabatic: Q=0, PVγ=constTVγ⁻¹=const; Adiabatic steeper than isothermalFree expansion: ΔU=0, W=0
›
Isothermal Process DetailsIsothermal process: T=constant → ΔT=0 → ΔU=0. Boyle's law: PV=constant, P1V1=P2V2. Conditions: perfectly conducting walls; infinitely slow process. Specific heat = ∞ (C = Q/mΔT = ∞ since ΔT=0). Isothermal elasticity Eθ = P. Work done: W = μRT loge(Vf/Vi) = μRT loge(Pi/Pf). FLOT: ΔQ = ΔW (all absorbed heat goes into work). PV graph: rectangular hyperbola; isotherms at different temperatures are parallel, never intersect; T1 < T2 < T3 (higher T gives higher isotherm). Slope = −P/V. Area between isotherm and volume axis = work done.
›
Adiabatic Process and Free ExpansionAdiabatic process: Q=0. Conditions: perfectly insulating walls; sudden/rapid process. FLOT: ΔU = −ΔW. If expansion (W>0): ΔU<0, temperature falls — adiabatic expansion produces cooling. If compression (W<0): ΔU>0, temperature rises — adiabatic compression produces heating. Poisson's law: PVγ=constant; TVγ−1=constant; Tγ/Pγ−1=constant. γ=Cp/Cv. Work done: W = (PiVi − PfVf)/(γ−1) = μR(Ti−Tf)/(γ−1). Adiabatic elasticity Eφ = γP. Specific heat = 0 (Q=0). Slope of adiabatic curve = −γ(P/V) = γ × slope of isothermal curve → adiabatic is always steeper than isothermal on PV graph. Examples: sudden tyre bursting; propagation of sound; expansion of steam in cylinder. Free expansion: both Q=0 and W=0, so ΔU=0 — temperature unchanged for ideal gas.

3) Second Law and Thermodynamic Cycles

Second Law: Clausius (heat cannot spontaneously flow from cold to hot body), Kelvin-Planck (no engine can have 100% efficiency). Heat engine: efficiency η = W/Q1 = (Q1−Q2)/Q1 = 1−Q2/Q1; always less than 1. Cyclic process: ΔU=0, Q=W; net work = area enclosed in PV diagram. Reversible vs irreversible processes. Entropy: dS = dQ/T; disorder measure; increases for irreversible processes.

Clausius: heat cold→hot needs workKelvin-Planck: η<100% alwaysHeat engine: η=1−Q₂/Q₁Entropy: dS=dQ/T
›
Second Law Statements and Heat EngineSecond Law statements — Clausius: impossible for a self-acting machine to transfer heat from cold to hot body without external agency. Kelvin's: impossible to cool a body below coldest surroundings to do continuous work. Kelvin-Planck: impossible to extract heat and fully convert to work without producing other effect. Consequence: efficiency of any heat engine is always less than unity. Heat engine: working substance absorbs Q1 from source at T1, does work W, rejects Q2 to sink at T2. Efficiency η = W/Q1 = (Q1−Q2)/Q1 = 1 − Q2/Q1. Perfect engine (η=1) is impossible. For cyclic process: ΔU=0, so ΔQ=ΔW; net work = area enclosed by closed curve on PV diagram; clockwise=positive, anticlockwise=negative.
›
Entropy and Reversible ProcessesEntropy: dS = dQ/T; measure of disorder of molecular motion. Greater disorder = greater entropy. At equilibrium, entropy is maximum. For state change at constant T: dS = ±mL/T (positive for absorption, negative for evolution). Temperature change: ΔS = mc loge(T2/T1) = 2.303 mc log10(T2/T1). For ideal gas: ΔS = μCv loge(T2/T1) + μR loge(V2/V1). Entropy is the mathematical form of Second Law. Reversible process: conditions include no dissipative forces, infinitely slow process, temperature difference between system and surroundings is negligible. All isothermal and adiabatic processes are reversible if performed very slowly. Irreversible processes: all natural processes — conduction, radiation, radioactive decay, free expansion, Joule-Thomson expansion. Irreversibility can be measured by entropy increase.

4) Carnot Engine and Efficiency

Carnot engine: theoretical ideal engine with maximum possible efficiency η = 1 − T2/T1 (T in Kelvin). Four strokes: isothermal expansion at T1, adiabatic expansion T1→T2, isothermal compression at T2, adiabatic compression T2→T1. No real engine can exceed Carnot efficiency. Refrigerator (reverse Carnot): COP β = Q2/W = Q2/(Q1−Q2) = T2/(T1−T2). Relation: β = (1−η)/η.

η = 1 − T₂/T₁ (Kelvin only)COP β = T₂/(T₁−T₂)4 strokes: iso-expand, adi-expand, iso-compress, adi-compressNo engine exceeds Carnot efficiency
›
Carnot Cycle Four Strokes and Efficiency DerivationCarnot engine consists of: cylinder with non-conducting walls and conducting base; source at T1; sink at T2; non-conducting stand. Four strokes: (1) Isothermal expansion AB at T1: W1=Q1=RT1 loge(V2/V1); (2) Adiabatic expansion BC: temperature falls T1→T2, W2=R(T1−T2)/(γ−1); (3) Isothermal compression CD at T2: W3=Q2=RT2 loge(V3/V4); (4) Adiabatic compression DA: temperature rises T2→T1, W4=R(T1−T2)/(γ−1). Since W2=W4 (cancel), net work W=W1−W3=Q1−Q2 = area ABCD on PV diagram. Using adiabatic relation: V3/V4 = V2/V1, so loge(V3/V4) = loge(V2/V1). Efficiency: η = 1 − Q2/Q1 = 1 − T2loge(V3/V4)/[T1loge(V2/V1)] = 1 − T2/T1. Carnot theorem: no heat engine between two given temperatures can be more efficient than Carnot engine; all reversible engines between same temperatures have equal efficiency.
›
Refrigerator COP and Relation to EfficiencyRefrigerator = heat engine run in reverse. Working substance (refrigerant: liquid ammonia, freon) extracts Q2 from cold body at T2, external work W is done on it, releases Q1=W+Q2 to hot body at T1 (atmosphere). COP: β = Q2/W = Q2/(Q1−Q2). For Carnot refrigerator: Q1/Q2 = T1/T2, so β = T2/(T1−T2). β=0 when T2=0 K. Perfect refrigerator (W=0, β=∞) is impossible. Relation between COP and efficiency: β = (1−η)/η, or η = 1/(1+β). Practical efficiencies: steam engine 8–15%; petrol engine ~40%; diesel engine ~50–55%. Carnot efficiency is the upper bound — always greater than practical engine efficiency for same temperature limits.

Sign Convention — Work Done BY vs ON System in First Law
NEET 2019NEET 2022First LawSign conventionHigh frequency trap

Mistake Snapshot (What Students Do Wrong)

  • Taking W = work done ON system as positive in ΔQ = ΔU + ΔW:: NCERT and standard Indian textbooks define ΔW as work done BY the system, positive for expansion. Some sources (especially international thermodynamics texts) define positive W as work done ON the system and write ΔQ = ΔU − W. Mixing these conventions mid-solution — using expansion-positive for W but then treating compression as positive ΔW — gives wrong signs for ΔQ and ΔU. All NEET solutions must use: expansion → W positive; heat absorbed → Q positive.
  • Forgetting that ΔW = 0 in isochoric process and writing Q = ΔU + W = ΔU + PΔV ≠ 0:: In an isochoric (constant volume) process, ΔV=0 → W=P·ΔV=0 always, regardless of pressure change. Students who write W=PΔV and then substitute ΔP·V or P·ΔV with a non-zero pressure change get wrong W. The volume does not change, so no PdV work is done.
2–3 Line Example (Typical Error)

1 mole of gas compressed isothermally. Work done ON gas = 500 J. ΔU=0 (isothermal). FLOT: ΔQ = ΔU + ΔW. Here W = work done BY gas = −500 J (compression). So ΔQ = 0 + (−500) = −500 J. Heat is released by gas. WRONG if student treats W = +500 J: ΔQ = 0 + 500 = +500 J (heat absorbed — wrong physical interpretation).

How NEET Frames The Trap

NEET writes 'work done on the gas is 200 J' then asks for heat exchange. Trap: student substitutes W = +200 J instead of W = −200 J (work done BY gas = −200 J). This flips the sign of ΔQ.

NEET-Style Trap Question Format

Q. 200 J of work is done on a system and 150 J of heat is absorbed by it. What is the change in internal energy of the system?
A. 50 J   B. 350 J   C. −50 J   D. 150 J  
Trick: Work done ON system = 200 J → work done BY system W = −200 J. ΔQ = +150 J (absorbed). FLOT: ΔU = ΔQ − ΔW = 150 − (−200) = 350 J. Answer B. Common wrong answer: 150 − 200 = −50 J (treats work on system as positive W in FLOT directly).

Quick rule: In FLOT, W = work done BY system. If problem says 'work done ON the system = X', substitute W = −X. If gas expands, W is positive. If compressed, W is negative. Never skip the sign flip when converting 'work on' to 'work by'.
Adiabatic Process — Q=0 Does NOT Mean Temperature is Constant
NEET 2018NEET 2021AdiabaticTemperature changeConceptual trap

Mistake Snapshot (What Students Do Wrong)

  • Claiming ΔT = 0 because Q = 0 in adiabatic process:: Q=0 means no heat exchange with surroundings. It does NOT constrain temperature. ΔU = −W: if gas expands (W>0), ΔU<0 and temperature falls. If gas is compressed (W<0), ΔU>0 and temperature rises. Temperature changes significantly in adiabatic processes — this is precisely why sudden tyre bursting causes cooling, and why adiabatic compression in a diesel engine is hot enough to ignite fuel.
  • Using ideal gas equation with T = constant for adiabatic process (applying Boyle's law PV=const instead of Poisson's law PVγ=const):: Boyle's law PV=constant applies ONLY to isothermal processes. For adiabatic processes the correct relation is PVγ=constant. Since γ>1, pressure falls faster with volume increase in adiabatic than in isothermal, which is why the adiabatic curve is steeper. Using wrong law gives wrong P or V ratios.
2–3 Line Example (Typical Error)

Gas expands adiabatically. Q=0, W=400 J (expansion). FLOT: ΔU = Q − W = 0 − 400 = −400 J. Since ΔU = μCvΔT, temperature decreases. WRONG claim: Q=0 → ΔU=0 → ΔT=0. Correct: Q=0 means ΔU=−W = −400 J; temperature falls.

How NEET Frames The Trap

NEET asks: 'In an adiabatic expansion of an ideal gas, which of the following is correct?' Trap options include 'Temperature remains constant' or 'Internal energy is unchanged'. Both are wrong.

NEET-Style Trap Question Format

Q. An ideal gas undergoes adiabatic compression. Which of the following is correct?
A. Temperature decreases and internal energy decreases   B. Temperature increases and internal energy increases   C. Temperature remains constant   D. Heat is absorbed from surroundings  
Trick: Adiabatic compression: Q=0, W<0 (work done ON gas). FLOT: ΔU = 0 − W = −(negative) = positive. ΔU>0 → ΔT>0 → temperature increases. Internal energy increases. Answer B. Option C is the isothermal result wrongly applied to adiabatic. Option D violates Q=0.

Quick rule: Adiabatic expansion → cooling (ΔU = −W, W>0). Adiabatic compression → heating (ΔU = −W, W<0). Temperature ALWAYS changes in adiabatic process. Q=0 is about heat exchange, not temperature constancy — temperature constancy is isothermal.
Carnot Efficiency — Celsius vs Kelvin Temperature
NEET 2016NEET 2019NEET 2023Carnot engineTemperature unit trapHighest frequency trap

Mistake Snapshot (What Students Do Wrong)

  • Substituting temperatures in Celsius directly into η = 1 − T2/T1:: η = 1 − T2/T1 is derived from the ratio of heat exchanged in isothermal processes, which involves absolute (Kelvin) temperature only. Using Celsius gives drastically wrong answers. Example: source at 127°C (400 K), sink at 27°C (300 K). Correct η = 1 − 300/400 = 0.25 = 25%. Using Celsius: η = 1 − 27/127 ≈ 0.79 = 79% — completely wrong. NEET systematically includes the Celsius-based wrong answer as an option.
  • Using COP formula β = T2/T1 instead of β = T2/(T1 − T2):: COP of Carnot refrigerator is β = T2/(T1−T2), where the denominator is the temperature difference, not T1 alone. Students who misremember write β = T2/T1 which equals 1−η (a different quantity). Since T1−T2 < T1, the correct β is always greater than T2/T1.
2–3 Line Example (Typical Error)

Source at 500°C, sink at 25°C. η = 1 − (25+273)/(500+273) = 1 − 298/773 ≈ 1 − 0.386 = 0.614 = 61.4%. Wrong (Celsius): η = 1 − 25/500 = 0.95 = 95%. NEET includes 95% as a distractor. Always add 273 first.

How NEET Frames The Trap

NEET states 'source temperature 627°C and sink temperature 27°C, find efficiency' then lists options that include both the Kelvin-correct answer and the Celsius-substituted wrong answer. The Celsius answer looks more 'exact' which makes it tempting.

NEET-Style Trap Question Format

Q. A Carnot engine operates between a source at 227°C and a sink at 27°C. What is the efficiency of the engine?
A. 40%   B. 87%   C. 60%   D. 13%  
Trick: T1 = 227+273 = 500 K; T2 = 27+273 = 300 K. η = 1 − 300/500 = 1 − 0.6 = 0.4 = 40%. Answer A. Option B (≈87%) is from using η = 1 − 27/227 (Celsius direct) — the classic trap. Convert to Kelvin first, always.

Quick rule: Carnot efficiency: always add 273 to both temperatures before substituting. Write 'T1 = __K, T2 = __K' as a mandatory first step. No Carnot calculation should begin without this conversion step. If the answer looks too high (>70%), recheck whether Celsius conversion was done.
PV Diagram Work — Clockwise vs Anticlockwise Sign and Stroke Areas
NEET 2017NEET 2020PV diagramCyclic processWork done area

Mistake Snapshot (What Students Do Wrong)

  • Taking net work = total area under all curves instead of area enclosed by the cycle:: For a cyclic process on a PV diagram, net work = area ENCLOSED by the closed loop, not the total shaded area below all curves. Total area below each curve (including the return path) cancels partially. Only the enclosed area between the forward and return paths represents net work. This is a vector subtraction: expansion strokes (upper path) minus compression strokes (lower path).
  • Assigning wrong sign to net work based on visual guess instead of direction of traversal:: Net work is positive when the cycle is traversed clockwise on a PV diagram (forward expansion at higher pressure, return compression at lower pressure). Net work is negative when anticlockwise. Students sometimes confuse 'right-side larger' with 'positive' — the direction of traversal (clockwise vs anticlockwise) is the sole determinant of sign.
2–3 Line Example (Typical Error)

Rectangular PV cycle: expansion at P2 from V1 to V2 (upper path), then isochoric drop P2→P1 at V2, then compression at P1 from V2 to V1 (lower path), then isochoric rise. Net W = W_expansion − W_compression = P2(V2−V1) − P1(V2−V1) = (P2−P1)(V2−V1) = area of rectangle. Clockwise traversal → positive net work.

How NEET Frames The Trap

NEET draws a closed rectangular PV cycle and asks 'what is the work done in the cycle?' Trap answer: students add the area under the upper path (large positive) without subtracting the area under the return path (large negative), getting a number twice as large.

NEET-Style Trap Question Format

Q. A gas undergoes a cyclic process on a PV diagram with P1=10⁵ Pa, P2=3×10⁵ Pa, V1=10⁻³ m³, V2=3×10⁻³ m³, traversed clockwise. What is the net work done?
A. 4×10² J   B. 4 J   C. 8×10² J   D. 2×10² J  
Trick: Net work = area enclosed = (P2−P1)(V2−V1) = (3−1)×10⁵ × (3−1)×10⁻³ = 2×10⁵ × 2×10⁻³ = 4×10² J = 400 J. Answer A. Clockwise → positive. Option C doubles the area by summing both upper and lower paths without subtraction.

Quick rule: Cyclic PV diagram: net work = enclosed area only. Clockwise = positive net work. Anticlockwise = negative net work. Do NOT sum areas under all individual paths — only the loop area counts as net work. For a rectangle: net W = (P2−P1)(V2−V1).
