Lami's Theorem – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

Lami's Theorem provides a direct algebraic tool for finding unknown forces when exactly three concurrent coplanar forces act on a body in static equilibrium. Each force is proportional to the sine of the angle between the other two: F₁/sinα = F₂/sinβ = F₃/sinγ. The theorem is a restatement of the triangle law of forces in trigonometric ratio form and is particularly powerful in NEET problems involving strings, ropes, and inclined supports at non-standard angles.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Draw the Force Triangle For any three-force equilibrium, first identify all three forces and draw them head-to-tail. The closing side of the triangle confirms equilibrium and validates your angle readings.

2

Label Angles Correctly The angle α in Lami’s theorem is the angle between forces F₂ and F₃ (NOT the angle F₁ makes with any axis). A common trap: NEET gives the angle each force makes with the vertical — you must convert to inter-force angles (they sum to 360°).

3

Apply the Ratio Write F₁/sinα = F₂/sinβ = F₃/sinγ. Plug the two known forces and their angles to solve for the unknown. Cross-check: all three ratios must equal the same constant.

Rapid Revision Cards

Concept → Trap → Example

1) Theorem Statement

Must Memorise

For three concurrent coplanar forces in equilibrium: each force divided by the sine of the angle between the other two equals a constant.

  • F₁/sinα = F₂/sinβ = F₃/sinγ
  • α + β + γ = 360°
  • Only valid for exactly THREE concurrent forces in equilibrium
Example (NEET-style)A 10 N block hangs from two strings making 30° and 60° with the vertical. Lami’s theorem gives T₁/sin150° = T₂/sin120° = 10/sin90°, yielding T₁ = 5 N and T₂ = 8.66 N.

2) Angle Convention

Common Trap

The angle α is between forces F₂ and F₃ — it is the angle OPPOSITE to F₁ in the triangle, not the angle F₁ makes with a reference axis.

  • If two strings make angles θ and φ with the vertical, the inter-string angle is θ + φ
  • Weight W acts straight down; its opposite angle = 180° − (θ + φ)
  • NEET papers often give angles with vertical, not inter-force angles
Example (NEET-style)Two strings at 30° and 60° with vertical: inter-string angle = 90°; angle opposite W = 90°; sin 90° = 1, so W = T₁·sin(complement of 60°) + T₂·sin(complement of 30°).

US Curriculum Gaps

Topics covered differently or not at all in AP Physics vs NEET.

Lami’s Theorem Not in AP Syllabus

US AP Physics 1 and C curricula solve three-force equilibrium exclusively by component resolution (Fx = 0, Fy = 0). Lami’s theorem as a named shortcut is absent.

  • Practice applying the theorem to avoid falling back on the slower component method in a timed exam.
  • Both methods give the same answer; Lami’s theorem is 40–60% faster for symmetric setups.

Angle Labelling Convention Differs

AP problems specify angles from the horizontal or vertical. NEET problems may give included angles directly or require conversion from axis-referenced angles.

  • Always draw the force diagram and label the three inter-force angles before applying the formula.
  • Verify that the three angles you label sum to 360°.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Can Lami’s theorem be applied to four or more forces?
No. Lami’s theorem is derived from the triangle law of forces and applies exclusively to three concurrent forces. For four or more forces, resolve into components or use Lami’s after combining any two forces into their resultant.
What if the included angle between two forces is greater than 180°?
This happens when a force is directed ‘outward’ from the equilibrium point. In such cases reflex angles are involved; sin of a reflex angle equals the sine of its supplement. The theorem still holds with careful sign conventions.
How is Lami’s theorem related to the sine rule in triangles?
When three forces in equilibrium are drawn head-to-tail they form a closed triangle. The sine rule for that force triangle is exactly Lami’s theorem. The angle in the force triangle between two sides equals 180° minus the inter-force angle, and sin(180° − θ) = sinθ.
Is Lami’s theorem valid in 3D (non-coplanar forces)?
No. Lami’s theorem requires the three forces to be coplanar. For 3D concurrent force equilibrium, use three simultaneous component equations (Fx = Fy = Fz = 0).
