Newton's Third Law of Motion – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

Newton’s Third Law states that to every action there is always an equal and opposite reaction. The action force Fₐₙ exerted on body A by body B, and the reaction force Fₙₐ exerted on B by A, are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and simultaneous. Critically, they act on DIFFERENT bodies — they can never cancel each other. This distinction is the single most tested conceptual trap in the entire Newton’s Laws chapter in NEET.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Always Draw Two FBDs For every action-reaction question, draw a separate Free Body Diagram for each body. Mark the action force on one body and the corresponding reaction on the other. This mechanically prevents the classic error of thinking N and mg cancel.

2

Three Tests for Action-Reaction Pairs An action-reaction pair must: (1) have equal magnitudes, (2) have opposite directions, (3) act on DIFFERENT bodies. If forces act on the same body, they are NOT an action-reaction pair regardless of their magnitudes.

3

Learn the Classic N vs mg trap N (normal reaction of table on book) and mg (weight of book due to Earth) both act on the book — same body → NOT action-reaction. The correct pairs are: (mg, pull of book on Earth) and (N, push of book on table).

Rapid Revision Cards

Concept → Trap → Example

1) The Law

Must Memorise

To every action, there is always an equal (in magnitude) and opposite (in direction) reaction. Fₐₙ = −Fₙₐ

  • Forces always occur in pairs; a single isolated force is impossible
  • Action and reaction act on DIFFERENT bodies — they never cancel
  • The two forces are simultaneous; neither is the cause of the other
Example (NEET-style)A gun of mass M fires a bullet of mass m at velocity v. Reaction: gun recoils at V = −mv/M. The bullet’s forward force (action on gun) and gun’s backward force (reaction on bullet) are equal in magnitude.

2) Identifying Action-Reaction Pairs

Exam Pattern

Action and reaction: (1) equal magnitude, (2) opposite direction, (3) act on different bodies, (4) same type of force (both contact or both gravitational).

  • Book on table: (a) Earth pulls book down [gravity] ↔ book pulls Earth up [gravity] — action-reaction pair
  • Book on table: (b) Table pushes book up [normal] ↔ book pushes table down [normal] — action-reaction pair
  • N and mg on the book are NOT a pair — both act on the book (different natures, same body)
Example (NEET-style)Swimming: swimmer pushes water backward (action). Water pushes swimmer forward (reaction). Both are contact forces; they act on different bodies (water and swimmer).

US Curriculum Gaps

Points where AP Physics treatment differs from the NEET conceptual emphasis.

NEET Focuses on Pair Identification

US AP Physics uses Newton’s Third Law primarily in calculations (Atwood machine, connected blocks). NEET additionally tests conceptual identification of pairs in statement-based (assertion-reason) MCQs.

  • Practice statement-based MCQs specifically: ‘Statement I: action and reaction cancel each other’ — this is FALSE.
  • Prepare for questions that give a scenario and ask which pair is the correct action-reaction pair from four options.

Previous Year Questions

NEET PYQs
1A book is lying on a table. The action-reaction pair for the force of gravity on the book is:NEET 2023
The normal force the table exerts on the book
The gravitational pull the book exerts on the Earth
The normal force the book exerts on the table
The weight of the table itself
The action force is the gravitational pull of the Earth on the book (mg downward, acts on the book). The reaction, by Newton’s Third Law, is the gravitational pull of the book on the Earth (equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, acts on the Earth). The normal force pair is a separate action-reaction pair: book pushes table down ↔ table pushes book up.
2Newton’s third law of motion is Fₐₙ = −Fₙₐ. Which statement about this is INCORRECT?NEET 2022
The two forces are equal in magnitude
The two forces act on different bodies
The two forces cancel each other making the net force on the system zero
The forces are of the same nature (both contact or both gravitational)
The statement that action and reaction cancel each other is incorrect. They act on DIFFERENT bodies; a force can only cancel another force that acts on the SAME body. The internal forces of an action-reaction pair cancel at the SYSTEM level (Newton’s third-law pairs sum to zero in the total system momentum change), but they cannot cancel a force on a single body.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
If action and reaction are equal, why do they not cancel each other?
Forces cancel only if they act on the SAME body. Action and reaction act on DIFFERENT bodies, so they cannot cancel each other. To cancel a force, you need an equal and opposite force on the same body, not on another body.
Is Newton’s Third Law always valid?
In classical mechanics (speeds much less than c and non-quantum systems), the third law holds universally. At relativistic speeds or in quantum electrodynamics, the instantaneous action-reaction picture breaks down because interactions propagate at finite speed through fields, and momentum is carried by the field itself.
Does Newton’s Third Law apply to non-contact forces like gravity?
Yes. The Earth pulls the Moon with a gravitational force, and the Moon pulls the Earth with an equal and opposite gravitational force. These are a valid action-reaction pair even without physical contact.
Can we identify which force is the ‘action’ and which is the ‘reaction’?
No. Newton’s third law is symmetric; neither force is more fundamental. The labels ‘action’ and ‘reaction’ are arbitrary. You can call either force the action; the other is then the reaction. What matters is that they are simultaneous, equal, opposite, and on different bodies.
