Newton's Second Law of Motion – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

Newton's Second Law states that the rate of change of linear momentum of a body is directly proportional to the applied force and occurs in the direction of the force. The fundamental form is F = dp/dt; for constant mass this reduces to F = ma. This law gives the quantitative definition of force and is derivable to encompass the First and Third Laws. It is the most fundamental of Newton's three laws and the core engine of NEET Mechanics problems.

1) The Fundamental Statement

Must Know

The rate of change of linear momentum of a body is directly proportional to the external force applied on the body, and this change takes place always in the direction of the applied force.

  • F = dp/dt (general form — works even when mass changes)
  • F = ma (derived when mass is constant)
  • Force and acceleration are in the same direction
Example (NEET-style)A 5 kg block accelerated at 3 m/s² requires a net force of F = 5 × 3 = 15 N in the direction of acceleration.

2) Derivation: F = dp/dt to F = ma

Derivation

F = dp/dt = d(mv)/dt = m(dv/dt) = ma (when mass m is constant). The constant K = 1 in both SI and CGS units by definition of unit force.

  • SI unit of force: 1 Newton = 1 kg × 1 m/s²
  • CGS unit: 1 dyne = 1 g × 1 cm/s²
  • 1 N = 10⁵ dyne
Example (NEET-style)One Newton is defined as the force that produces 1 m/s² acceleration in a 1 kg mass. This is the defining choice that makes K = 1.

3) Second Law as Most Fundamental

Conceptual

Newton's Second Law is the most fundamental of the three laws because both the First and Third Laws can be derived from it.

  • First Law: set F = 0 in F = ma → a = 0 (constant velocity)
  • Second Law gives quantitative definition of force
  • Third Law derivable using isolated systems and momentum conservation
Example (NEET-style)If two interacting bodies form an isolated system, F_net = dp_total/dt = 0 (no external force), so F₁₂ = −F₂₁ — this is Newton's Third Law derived from the Second.

4) Impulse-Momentum Theorem

Key Application

F·Δt = Δp = m(v − u). The impulse of a force equals the change in momentum. Valid for constant or average force over Δt.

  • Large force, small time ≈ impulsive force (bat hitting ball, explosion)
  • Area under F–t graph = impulse = change in momentum
  • Unit of impulse = N·s = kg·m/s (same as momentum unit)
Example (NEET-style)A cricket bat exerts 500 N on a 0.16 kg ball for 0.01 s. Impulse = 500 × 0.01 = 5 N·s. Ball gains momentum 5 kg·m/s, so velocity gain = 5/0.16 = 31.25 m/s.

Curriculum Bridging — US vs NEET

Key differences in Newton's Second Law treatment between NEET and US AP Physics.

F = dp/dt vs F = ma

US AP Physics uses F = ma as the primary form. NEET teaches F = dp/dt as the fundamental form and derives F = ma.

  • Know F = dp/dt handles variable mass (rocket propulsion, rain accumulation on trolley)
  • F = ma only valid for constant mass systems — explicitly required in NEET

Second Law as Most Fundamental

NEET emphasises that Newton's Second Law is the most fundamental because First and Third Laws derive from it.

  • KImportant MCQ: 'Which is most fundamental?' Answer: Second Law
  • First Law is a special case (F = 0) of the Second Law

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why is F = ma only valid for constant mass?
The general Second Law is F = dp/dt = d(mv)/dt. When mass m is constant, this becomes m·(dv/dt) = ma. For systems where mass changes with time (rockets expelling gas, raindrops accumulating on a trolley), you must use F = dp/dt and account for the mass change term. F = ma without correction gives wrong answers in those cases.
How is the First Law a special case of the Second Law?
If the net force F = 0, then from F = ma: a = F/m = 0/m = 0. Zero acceleration means constant velocity (which includes being at rest). This is precisely Newton's First Law. The Second Law thus contains the First Law as its F = 0 limit.
What is the difference between force and impulse?
Force (F) is the rate of change of momentum — it is an instantaneous or sustained quantity measured in Newtons. Impulse (J = F·Δt) is the total change in momentum delivered by a force over a time interval, measured in N·s (= kg·m/s). Impulse is what you integrate under the force-time curve.
