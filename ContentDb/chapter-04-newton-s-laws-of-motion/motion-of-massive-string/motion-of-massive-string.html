Motion of Massive String – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

Unlike a massless string (where tension is uniform throughout), a massive string has a varying tension at each cross-section. When a force F is applied to a string of mass m and length L, the tension at a point distance x from the force end is Tₓ = F(L−x)/L. This is a direct consequence of Newton’s Second Law applied to the string segment beyond x. NEET tests the tension formula and the ability to identify the correct fraction.

Preparation Strategy

1

Fix the Formula Tₓ = F(L−x)/L This follows from applying F = ma to the string segment of length (L−x) that must be accelerated. That segment has mass m(L−x)/L, and the whole string accelerates at a = F/m. So Tₓ = [m(L−x)/L] × (F/m) = F(L−x)/L.

2

Study All Four Cases from Table 4.3 Case 1: uniform string on frictionless surface. Case 2: force at one end, tension varies. Case 3: two opposing forces. Case 4: string hanging vertically. Each case has a different acceleration and tension formula.

3

Note the Endpoints At x = 0 (force end): T = F (maximum). At x = L (free end): T = 0. These boundary conditions serve as quick checks.

Quick Revision Cards

Concept → Trap → Example

1) Position-Dependent Tension — Core Formula

Must Know

String mass m, length L, force F applied at one end. Acceleration a = F/m. Segment beyond x has mass Mₓ = m(L−x)/L. Newton’s 2nd Law on that segment: Tₓ = Mₓ × a = m(L−x)/L × F/m.

  • Tₓ = F(L−x)/L where x is measured from the applied-force end
  • Tₓ decreases linearly from F (at x=0) to 0 (at x=L)
  • Tension at midpoint: F/2
Example (NEET-style)String mass 2 kg, length 4 m, force 20 N. Tension 1 m from force end: T = 20×(4−1)/4 = 15 N.

2) Vertically Hanging Massive String

NEET Trap

String of mass m, length L hangs from a fixed support. No applied force. Tension at height h from bottom: Tₕ = mg∕L × h (weight of string below point h supports that segment). Maximum tension at the top (h = L): T = mg.

  • Tension at the top = mg (full weight of string)
  • Tension at the bottom = 0
  • Tension at midpoint = mg/2
Example (NEET-style)String mass 1 kg, length 2 m. Tension at 0.5 m from bottom: T = (1×10/2)×0.5 = 2.5 N.

3) Two Opposing Forces (Table 4.3 Case 3)

Advanced

Forces F₁ and F₂ applied at two ends of the string, F₂ > F₁. Net acceleration a = (F₂−F₁)/m. Tension at distance x from F₂ end: Tₓ = F₂ − m(F₂−F₁)/m × x/L = F₂ − (F₂−F₁)x/L.

  • When F₁ = F₂: a = 0, tension is uniform throughout = F₁ = F₂
  • This checks out: no net motion, string in static equilibrium at every cross-section
  • Tension varies linearly from F₂ at one end to F₁ at the other
Example (NEET-style)F₂ = 30 N, F₁ = 10 N, L = 5 m. Tension at x = 2.5 m from F₂ end: T = 30 − (20/5)×2.5 = 30 − 10 = 20 N.

US Curriculum Bridging

US physics almost always uses inextensible massless strings. Massive strings with position-varying tension are essentially not covered in US AP Physics 1 or Physics C.

Position-Dependent Tension — New Concept

In massless string problems, tension is the same everywhere. In massive string problems, each cross-section bears only the weight/force of the portion ahead of it. NEET tests this concept through Table 4.3.

  • Always isolate the segment beyond the point of interest; apply Newton’s 2nd Law to that segment.
  • Practice checking endpoints: at the free end, T = 0; at the applied-force end, T = F.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why does tension decrease from the force end to the free end?
The end where force is applied must accelerate the entire string. As you move toward the free end, you are pulling less and less mass. With less mass to accelerate, Less force (tension) is needed. At the very free end, there is no string ahead to pull, so T = 0.
What is the difference between tension in a massive vs. massless string?
Massless string: tension is the same throughout (T is constant). Massive string: tension varies at every cross-section. T decreases from the pulled end to the free end. This is why NEET asks ‘find tension at point P’ only when the string has significant mass.
Does the massive string formula apply when the string is accelerating vertically?
Yes, but you must include gravity. For a vertical string being pulled upward with acceleration a, the tension at height h from the bottom is T = m(a+g)h/L. For free fall (a = −g), tension everywhere = 0, which makes physical sense.
