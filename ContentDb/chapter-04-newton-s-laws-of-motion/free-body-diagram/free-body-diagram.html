Free Body Diagram – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

A Free Body Diagram (FBD) isolates a single object from its surroundings and replaces all interactions with explicit force vectors. Writing the correct FBD is the first step to solving every Newton’s Laws problem in NEET. The method: choose the object, identify all forces (gravity mg, normal N, tension T, friction f, applied F), draw vectors on the isolated body, choose axes, then write ∑F = ma for each axis.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Learn the 4-Step FBD Method Step 1: Identify the object of interest and isolate it. Step 2: Identify and draw ALL forces acting on it (gravity, normal, tension, friction, applied). Step 3: Choose coordinate axes (align one axis with likely direction of acceleration). Step 4: Write ΣFx = ma_x and ΣFy = ma_y.

2

Master the 6 Common Force Types For NEET problems you need: (1) Weight mg (always downward), (2) Normal force N (perpendicular to surface, away from surface), (3) Tension T (along rope, away from object), (4) Friction f (opposing relative motion or tendency), (5) Applied force F (given direction), (6) Pseudo force F_pseudo = ma (for non-inertial frames, directed opposite to frame acceleration).

3

Apply to Every Problem Type Draw a separate FBD for each block in connected-block problems. Do not mix forces on different blocks. The equation for block on inclined plane: along incline: F − mg sinθ − f = ma; perpendicular: N − mg cosθ = 0.

Concept → Trap → Example

1) The FBD Procedure

Core Method

Isolate the object. Draw all external forces acting ON it. Choose axes. Write ΣF = ma for each direction.

  • Only forces acting ON the object are drawn (not forces the object exerts on others)
  • Normal force is always perpendicular to the contact surface, pointing away from the surface
  • Tension in a string is always directed along the string, away from the object
  • Friction opposes relative motion (kinetic) or tendency of motion (static)
  • Weight mg is always vertical downward, regardless of surface orientation
Example (NEET-style)Block on smooth horizontal surface with applied horizontal force F: FBD shows F (right), N (up), mg (down). ΣFy = 0 ⇒ N = mg. ΣFx = ma ⇒ F = ma ⇒ a = F/m.

2) FBD on Inclined Plane

High-frequency Problem

Tilt axes: x along incline (positive up the incline), y perpendicular to incline. Weight components: mg sinθ along incline (down the slope), mg cosθ perpendicular to incline.

  • N = mg cosθ (no net motion perpendicular to incline)
  • For smooth incline: ma = mg sinθ ⇒ a = g sinθ (down the slope)
  • For rough incline: friction f = μN = μ mg cosθ opposing motion
  • Net force down rough incline: mg sinθ − μ mg cosθ = mg(sinθ − μ cosθ)
Example (NEET-style)Block on incline at 30°, μ = 0.2, m = 2 kg, g = 10 m/s². N = 2×10×cos30° = 17.3 N. f = 0.2×17.3 = 3.46 N. Net force = 2×10×sin30° − 3.46 = 10 − 3.46 = 6.54 N. a = 6.54/2 = 3.27 m/s².

US Curriculum Gaps

How AP Physics approach to FBDs differs from NEET expectations.

NEET Tests FBD Through Connected Systems

NEET does not usually ask students to draw an FBD explicitly (multiple choice format). Instead, FBD knowledge is tested through getting correct tension, acceleration, and normal force values in 2-block systems, Atwood machines, and lift problems. US-educated students who draw detailed FBDs on exam day still need to verify their forces quickly.

  • For 2 blocks A and B with common acceleration: draw FBD for each block separately, write separate F=ma equations, then solve simultaneously.
  • Treat rope/string as massless and inextensible unless told otherwise.

Pseudo Force in Non-Inertial Frames

NEET includes problems where the observer is in an accelerating frame (lift, train turning). The pseudo force = ma (directed opposite to the frame’s acceleration) must be added to the FBD for valid Newton’s Law equations. AP Physics emphasizes inertial frame solutions.

  • Pseudo force F_pseudo = m × (acceleration of frame), direction opposite to frame’s acceleration.
  • Alternative: solve from an inertial frame without pseudo force — same answer, often simpler.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
What is the purpose of isolating the object in an FBD?
Isolation ensures you only include forces acting ON the chosen object. It prevents the common mistake of including forces the object exerts on others (action-reaction pairs must be drawn on different objects). Once isolated, Newton’s second law ΣF = ma applies cleanly to the object.
Do I need to draw FBDs for every problem in NEET?
For MCQ problems in a time-limited exam, you don’t always draw a full FBD. However, drawing even a quick sketch (2-3 seconds) prevents sign errors that lose 4 marks per question. The more complex the problem (connected blocks, pulleys), the more valuable a quick FBD sketch is.
When is friction included in an FBD?
Friction exists at any surface contact where there is relative motion or a tendency of relative motion. Include it whenever: (1) the surface is described as rough, (2) a coefficient of friction μ is given, or (3) the object is about to slide or is sliding. For smooth surfaces or frictionless conditions, friction is zero.
Why does normal force not always equal mg?
N = mg only when the surface is horizontal and the object is in vertical equilibrium with no vertical acceleration. On an incline, N = mg cosθ. In a lift, N changes with acceleration. When an applied force has a vertical component, N adjusts to maintain equilibrium perpendicular to the surface. Always derive N from the FBD for that specific configuration.
