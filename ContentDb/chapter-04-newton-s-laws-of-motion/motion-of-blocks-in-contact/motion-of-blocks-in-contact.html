Motion of Blocks in Contact – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

Analyses two or three blocks pushed together (direct contact, no string) on a smooth horizontal surface. By treating the entire system together and then isolating each body with a FBD, the common acceleration a = F/(m₁+m₂) and the contact force (normal force between blocks) N = m₂F/(m₁+m₂) are derived. The key NEET insight: the contact force transmitted between blocks is always less than the applied force F.

Preparation Strategy

1

System-First Approach for Acceleration Always compute acceleration first by treating all blocks as one system: a = F / (m₁ + m₂ + ... + mn). This uses Newton’s 2nd Law for the whole without needing individual contact forces.

2

Isolate One Block for Contact Force To find f between blocks A and B, draw a FBD of the single block farthest from the applied force. The only horizontal force on that block is the contact force pushing it forward. Set f = (mass of isolated block) × a.

3

Direction of Applied Force Matters If force F is applied to the left block (A), contact force between A and B: f = m₂ a = m₂ F/(m₁+m₂). If F is applied to right block (B), reverse the formula: f = m₁ a = m₁ F/(m₁+m₂).

Quick Revision Cards

Concept → Trap → Example

1) Two Blocks — Force Applied to Block A

Core Formula

Force F applied to m₁, with m₂ in contact. System acceleration: a = F/(m₁+m₂). Contact force on m₂: f = m₂ × a = m₂ F/(m₁+m₂).

  • a = F/(m₁+m₂)
  • f = m₂ F / (m₁+m₂)
  • f is always less than F since m₂ < (m₁+m₂)
Example (NEET-style)m₁ = 3 kg, m₂ = 2 kg, F = 10 N: a = 10/5 = 2 m/s²; f = 2×2 = 4 N.

2) Two Blocks — Force Applied to Block B

Direction Switch

If F is applied on the right block (m₂) instead, the acceleration is the same: a = F/(m₁+m₂). Contact force on m₁ (isolated) = m₁ a = m₁ F/(m₁+m₂).

  • a = F/(m₁+m₂) (same as before)
  • f = m₁ F / (m₁+m₂) (now different from case 1!)
  • Swapping the direction of F swaps the numerator mass in the contact-force formula
Example (NEET-style)m₁ = 3 kg, m₂ = 2 kg, F = 10 N applied to m₂: f = 3×2 = 6 N (larger than case 1).

3) Three Blocks — f₁ and f₂

NEET Trap

F applied to m₁, blocks m₂ and m₃ in series behind it. Common: a = F/(m₁+m₂+m₃). Contact force between m₁ and m₂: f₁ = (m₂+m₃)F/(m₁+m₂+m₃). Contact force between m₂ and m₃: f₂ = m₃ F/(m₁+m₂+m₃).

  • f₁ = (m₂+m₃) F / (m₁+m₂+m₃)
  • f₂ = m₃ F / (m₁+m₂+m₃)
  • f₁ > f₂ because more mass is being pushed by the f₁ force
Example (NEET-style)m₁ = 1, m₂ = 2, m₃ = 3 kg, F = 12 N: a = 2 m/s²; f₁ = 5×2 = 10 N; f₂ = 3×2 = 6 N.

US Curriculum Bridging

Contact force between touching blocks requires systematic FBD isolation, which US AP Physics teaches but rarely tests for three-body cases.

Three-Body Contact Force Differentiation

US AP Physics C typically stops at two-body systems in contact force problems. NEET extends to three blocks and asks for a specific contact force between a specified pair.

  • Always draw a separate FBD for the block (or sub-group of blocks) on the side farthest from F.
  • The contact force = (sum of masses on that side) × a.

Force Direction Sensitivity

NEET explicitly tests both directions of F application on the same block stack. US curricula rarely vary this parameter in a practice problem.

  • Practice both F-from-left and F-from-right versions of the same two-block problem.
  • Notice that the contact force formula numerator contains only the mass(es) being pushed, not the mass being pulled.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why is the contact force between blocks less than the applied force F?
The contact force only needs to accelerate the remaining sub-system (blocks beyond the contact point). Since that sub-system has smaller mass than the total, f = (smaller mass) × a < F = (total mass) × a.
Why does the contact force formula change when F switches direction?
The contact force acts on the sub-system on one particular side of the contact. Which sub-system that is depends on the direction of F — it is always the sub-system that the contact force is pushing. Different direction, different sub-system, different formula.
Can the surface have friction in this topic?
In this OCR-grounded topic, the surface is smooth (frictionless). All derivations assume zero friction. With friction, each block’s FBD must include a frictional force and the formulas change significantly.
