Acceleration of Block on Horizontal Surface – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

Applies Newton’s Second Law to a block on a frictionless horizontal surface under three force configurations: (1) horizontal pull — reaction equals weight, acceleration is F/m; (2) angled pull upward at θ — reaction reduces, net horizontal force drives acceleration; (3) angled push downward at θ — reaction increases. Recognising how the direction of applied force changes the normal reaction is the critical NEET skill for this topic.

Preparation Strategy

1

Master the FBD Draw a complete Free Body Diagram for every case. Mark R (reaction), mg (weight), and the applied force F with its components before writing any equation.

2

Case-by-Case Formula Practice For Case 2 (pull at angle θ upward): write R = mg − F sinθ and a = (F cosθ)/m. For Case 3 (push at angle θ downward): write R = mg + F sinθ and a = (F cosθ)/m. Drill these until they become reflex.

3

Check Normal Reaction Sign If the block is pulled upward, the vertical component of pull opposes gravity, so R decreases. If pushed downward, the push adds to gravity, so R increases. This sign test confirms your FBD is correct.

Quick Revision Cards

Concept → Trap → Example

1) Case 1 — Horizontal Pull

Formula Focus

When the applied force F is strictly horizontal on a frictionless surface: normal reaction R equals the weight mg, and acceleration a equals F/m.

  • R = mg (vertical equilibrium)
  • a = F / m (horizontal Newton’s 2nd law)
  • No component of F affects the vertical direction
Example (NEET-style)A 5 kg block on a smooth floor is pulled by a horizontal force of 20 N. Acceleration = 20/5 = 4 m/s²; reaction = 5×10 = 50 N.

2) Case 2 — Pull at Angle θ Upward

NEET Trap

When force F acts at angle θ above horizontal: the vertical component F sinθ acts upward, reducing the normal reaction. R = mg − F sinθ. Horizontal component drives acceleration: a = F cosθ / m.

  • R = mg − F sinθ (reaction decreases)
  • a = (F cosθ) / m
  • If F sinθ = mg, block lifts off (R = 0)
Example (NEET-style)F = 20 N at 30°, m = 2 kg, g = 10 m/s²: R = 2×10 − 20×0.5 = 10 N; a = 20×cos30°/2 = 8.66 m/s².

3) Case 3 — Push at Angle θ Downward

Formula Focus

When force F acts at angle θ below horizontal (a push): the vertical component F sinθ acts downward, adding to gravity and increasing the normal reaction. R = mg + F sinθ.

  • R = mg + F sinθ (reaction increases)
  • a = (F cosθ) / m
  • Block cannot lift off the surface in a push case
Example (NEET-style)F = 20 N pushed at 30° downward, m = 2 kg: R = 20 + 20×0.5 = 30 N; a = (20×0.866)/2 = 8.66 m/s².

US Curriculum Bridging

NEET emphasises systematic case analysis and exact sign of normal reaction, which AP Physics covers but with less structural rigour.

Normal Reaction Sign Convention

US AP Physics teaches the concept but rarely tests the sign change in R when force direction changes. NEET directly asks ‘what is the normal reaction when a block is pulled at angle θ’.

  • Practice computing R separately for pull-up vs push-down scenarios.
  • Always draw the vertical FBD component before writing the horizontal equation.

Frictionless Surface Assumption

Indian NEET syllabus isolates the frictionless case explicitly before introducing friction. US curricula mix friction in earlier, which can lead to over-complicating the basic FBD.

  • For this topic, set friction = 0 and practice pure F = ma analysis.
  • Friction is introduced separately in Chapter 5 (Friction) in the NEET syllabus.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why does pulling at an angle reduce the normal reaction?
The upward component of the pull (F sinθ) partially opposes gravity. Since the block is not accelerating vertically, the floor only needs to supply R = mg − F sinθ to balance the net downward force. The floor reaction decreases because the applied force is helping to support the block.
Why does pushing at an angle increase the normal reaction?
The downward component of the push (F sinθ) adds to the weight pressing the block against the floor. The floor must supply R = mg + F sinθ to maintain vertical equilibrium.
Does the acceleration formula change with direction of pull?
No. In all three cases, the net horizontal force driving the block is F cosθ, so a = F cosθ / m. For the horizontal pull (θ = 0), cosθ = 1, giving a = F/m.
What happens if F sinθ > mg for an angled pull?
The block would lift off the surface. Once R = 0, the block leaves the floor and enters projectile-like motion. NEET sometimes asks for the minimum angle at which this occurs.
