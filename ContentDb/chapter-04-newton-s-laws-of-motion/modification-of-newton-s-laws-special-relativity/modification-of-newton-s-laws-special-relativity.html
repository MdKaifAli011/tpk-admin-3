Modification of Newton’s Laws — Special Relativity – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

Newton’s laws assume absolute time and space and hold perfectly for velocities v ≪ c. When an object moves at speeds comparable to the speed of light, Einstein’s Special Theory of Relativity modifies three fundamental quantities: length contracts (L = L₀√(1−v²/c²)), time dilates (T = T₀/√(1−v²/c²)), and relativistic mass increases (m = m₀/√(1−v²/c²)). NEET tests the qualitative understanding and formula recall for these three effects.

Preparation Strategy

1

Understand the Newtonian Limitation Newton assumed time and space are absolute (independent of the observer). Einstein showed this is wrong for high speeds: two observers in relative motion measure different lengths and times for the same event.

2

Memorise All Three Formulas with Direction Length: L = L₀√(1−v²/c²) → decreases. Time: T = T₀/√(1−v²/c²) → increases (clock runs slow). Mass: m = m₀/√(1−v²/c²) → increases. The key insight: m and T scale with γ; L scales with 1/γ.

3

Apply Limiting Cases At v = 0.8c, compute √(1−0.64) = √0.36 = 0.6. So L = 0.6L₀ (40% contraction); T = T₀/0.6 (67% longer); m = m₀/0.6 (≉67% heavier). Practice with v = 0.6c and v = 0.8c.

Quick Revision Cards

Concept → Trap → Example

1) The Lorentz Factor γ

Foundation

The central quantity in special relativity is the Lorentz factor γ = 1/√(1−v²/c²). At low velocities (v ≪ c), γ ≈ 1 and all classical results are recovered. At v → c, γ → ∞ and relativistic effects become extreme.

  • γ ≥ 1 always
  • At v = 0.6c: γ = 1/√(1−0.36) = 1/0.8 = 1.25
  • At v = 0.8c: γ = 1/0.6 ≈ 1.67
  • Newton’s laws are valid when γ ≈ 1, i.e., v ≪ c
Example (NEET-style)v = 0 → γ = 1 (classical). v = 0.866c → γ = 2 (strong relativistic effects).

2) Length Contraction and Time Dilation

Must Know

According to OCR page 203: 'Newton’s laws are true, as long as we are dealing with velocities which are small compare to velocity of light.' At high v: moving rods appear shorter (length contraction) and moving clocks appear slower (time dilation).

  • Length Contraction: L = L₀√(1−v²/c²) = L₀/γ
  • Time Dilation: T = T₀/√(1−v²/c²) = γT₀
  • A moving clock appears to tick slower
  • A moving rod appears shorter along the direction of motion
Example (NEET-style)Rod of rest length 10 m moves at 0.6c. L = 10 × 0.8 = 8 m. Observer sees 8 m, not 10 m.

3) Relativistic Mass Increase

Must Know

From OCR page 204: 'Hence, the mass increases with increase in velocity.' Relativistic mass: m = m₀/√(1−v²/c²) = γm₀. This explains why no object with rest mass can reach or exceed the speed of light: mass → ∞ as v → c, requiring infinite force.

  • m = m₀/√(1−v²/c²) = γm₀
  • At v = 0.8c, m = m₀/0.6 ≈ 1.67m₀
  • At v = 0.99c, m ≈ 7.09m₀
  • F = ma becomes F = dp/dt in relativistic mechanics
Example (NEET-style)Rest mass 1 kg, v = 0.8c. m = 1/0.6 ≈ 1.67 kg. Relativistic momentum p = mv ≈ 1.67 × 0.8c.

US Curriculum Bridging

Special relativity is introduced in US Modern Physics courses but not in standard Mechanics (AP Physics 1 or C: Mechanics). The NEET framing as a ‘Newton’s Law modification’ topic is novel for US-trained students.

Relativistic Mass — Modern vs Classical

US physics sometimes uses the concept of relativistic momentum (p = γm₀v) but may avoid the term ‘relativistic mass’ as a separate concept. NEET uses m = m₀/√(1−v²/c²) directly.

  • Remember: for NEET, m = γm₀ is the operative formula.
  • This leads directly to: as v → c, m → ∞, making it impossible for a massive object to reach c.

Newtonian vs Einsteinian Framing

The NEET curriculum explicitly discusses this as a limitation of Newton’s laws, not just a new independent theory. From OCR page 203: ‘According to Newton, time and space are absolute’ — this framing is tested as a conceptual MCQ.

  • Know the statement: Newton assumed absolute time and space.
  • Know the correction: For v comparable to c, time and space are relative to the observer.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why is time dilation the reciprocal formula of length contraction?
Both arise from the same Lorentz factor, but they affect their respective quantities in opposite ways. Length along the direction of motion is multiplied by √(1−v²/c²) (reduces it). Time is divided by √(1−v²/c²) (increases it). These compensate each other to keep the spacetime interval invariant.
Does relativistic mass affect Newton’s F = ma?
Yes. In relativistic mechanics, F = dp/dt = d(γm₀v)/dt, which reduces to F = ma only when v ≪ c. The effective inertia of an object increases with speed, so the same force produces less acceleration as the object speeds up.
Why can’t any massive object reach the speed of light?
As v → c, the Lorentz factor γ → ∞. Since m = γm₀, the relativistic mass → ∞. To accelerate infinite mass requires infinite energy. Therefore, for any object with non-zero rest mass m₀, reaching c requires infinite energy — which is impossible.
