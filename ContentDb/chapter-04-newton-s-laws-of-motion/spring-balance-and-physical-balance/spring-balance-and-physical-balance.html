Spring Balance and Physical Balance – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

A spring balance measures apparent weight (reaction force), which changes in accelerating frames. A physical balance compares masses by equalising gravitational forces, so it is immune to non-inertial frame effects. NEET targets: (1) spring balance reading in accelerating lifts; (2) ‘false balance’ errors when pan masses are unequal — the true weight is the geometric mean W = √(W₁W₂) (unequal arm length) or arithmetic mean W = (W₁+W₂)/2 (equal arm, unequal pan masses).

Preparation Strategy

1

Spring Balance in an Elevator The reading R = m(g+a) when going up (or decelerating downward). R = m(g−a) when going down (or decelerating upward). R = 0 in free fall. These are identical to the apparent weight results from Topic 13 (Apparent Weight in Lift).

2

Perfect Physical Balance When a physical balance is perfect (equal arm lengths, equal pan masses), it compares gravitational weight directly. Being in a non-inertial frame does NOT affect the reading: both pans experience the same pseudo-force, so they cancel.

3

False Balance — Two Cases Case (a): Unequal arm lengths. Body weighed twice: W₁ and W₂. True weight W = √(W₁W₂) (geometric mean). Case (b): Equal arm lengths, unequal pan masses. Body weighed twice: W₁ and W₂. True weight W = (W₁+W₂)/2 (arithmetic mean).

Quick Revision Cards

Concept → Trap → Example

1) Spring Balance — All Four Cases

Must Know

From OCR page 203: 'Spring balance: When its upper end is fixed with rigid support and body of mass m hung from its lower end.' The reading equals the reaction force R, not the true weight.

  • Lift at rest or uniform velocity: R = mg
  • Lift accelerating upward: R = m(g+a)
  • Lift accelerating downward: R = m(g−a)
  • Lift in free fall: R = 0 (weightlessness)
Example (NEET-style)Body mass 10 kg, lift accelerating up at 2 m/s²: Spring balance reads R = 10×(10+2) = 120 N.

2) Physical Balance — Perfect vs False

NEET Concept

From OCR page 203: 'In physical balance actually we compare the mass of body in both the pans. Here we do not calculate the absolute weight of the body.' A perfect balance is unaffected by non-inertial frames: the pseudo-force on both sides is equal and cancels.

  • Perfect physical balance: frame-independent — reads correctly even in an accelerating lift
  • False balance: unequal arm lengths or unequal pan masses introduce reading error
  • Distinguishing feature: spring balance IS frame-dependent; physical balance is NOT (when perfect)
Example (NEET-style)Weighing on Earth vs Moon with a physical balance gives the same mass reading. With a spring balance, different readings (because g differs).

3) False Balance — True Weight Formulas

Must Know

From OCR page 203: 'When the masses of the pan are not equal then balance shows the error in measurement.' Two derivation cases give two different true-weight formulas.

  • Case (a): Unequal arm lengths L₁ ≠ L₂
  • Two measurements: W₁ (body in left pan), W₂ (body in right pan)
  • True weight W = √(W₁ × W₂) (geometric mean)
  • Case (b): Equal arm lengths, unequal pan masses m₁ ≠ m₂
  • True weight W = (W₁ + W₂) / 2 (arithmetic mean)
Example (NEET-style)Unequal arm: W₁ = 9 N, W₂ = 4 N. True weight = √(9×4) = √36 = 6 N.

US Curriculum Bridging

Spring balance (scale) in an elevator is standard US AP Physics. Physical balance and false weight derivations are unique to the Indian competitive exam curriculum.

False Balance — Geometric vs Arithmetic Mean

The false balance problem asks: if a balance has unequal arm lengths, what is the true mass? This requires deriving torque equilibrium equations for two configurations. US students will find the formula W = √(W₁W₂) completely new.

  • Unequal arm: L₁W = L₂W₁ and L₂W = L₁W₂. Multiply: W² = W₁W₂, so W = √(W₁W₂).
  • Unequal pan: (W+m₁g)L = m₂gL gives W₁; swapped gives W₂. Average: W = (W₁+W₂)/2.

Physical Balance Frame Independence

US students may not know that a physical balance (two-arm scale) is unaffected by the acceleration of the frame. This is because pseudo-force acts equally on both sides, leaving the torque balance unchanged.

  • In a lift accelerating at a, both pans experience pseudo-force m̀a downward. Balance condition: m₁L₁ = m₂L₂ is preserved. Reading unchanged.
  • This is why a physical balance measures mass (frame-independent), not weight (frame-dependent).

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why does a physical balance still work correctly in an accelerating lift?
A physical balance in equilibrium satisfies torque balance: m₁L = m₂L (equal arms). If the lift accelerates at a, the effective gravity becomes (g+a) or (g−a), but this factor multiplies both sides equally. The balance condition m₁ = m₂ is unchanged. So the balance measures the ratio of masses, not the local g.
Would a spring balance and physical balance give different readings on the Moon?
Yes. A spring balance reads weight (mg), which is lower on the Moon (g_Moon ≈ 1.67 m/s²). A physical balance compares masses, so it reads the same mass on the Moon as on Earth. This is the key conceptual difference for NEET.
In the false balance case (a), why is the result the geometric mean rather than the arithmetic mean?
Setting up torque equilibrium twice: W×L₁ = W₁×L₂ and W×L₂ = W₂×L₁. Multiply both equations: W²×L₁L₂ = W₁W₂×L₁L₂. Cancel L₁L₂: W² = W₁W₂, giving W = √(W₁W₂). This is the geometric mean by construction.
