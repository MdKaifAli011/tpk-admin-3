Impulse – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

Impulse quantifies the total effect of a force acting over time. Mathematically, J = ∫F dt = Fₐᵥ × Δt = Δp = m(v − u). The impulse-momentum theorem states that impulse equals the change in momentum. Its key exam consequence: for a fixed impulse J, increasing contact time Δt decreases the average force Fₐᵥ — the principle behind catching technique, crumple zones, and sporting gear design.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Memorise the Chain Formula J = ∫F dt = Fₐᵥ × Δt = Δp = m(v − u) = mv − mu. Every NEET impulse question reduces to one or two links in this chain.

2

F-t Graph Area = Impulse If a force-time graph is given, the area under the curve equals impulse. For a triangular F-t graph: J = ½ × base × height. For a rectangular graph: J = height × width. NEET gives varied shapes.

3

Apply Inverse F-Δt Relation to Scenarios J = constant means Fₐᵥ × Δt = constant. Increasing Δt decreases Fₐᵥ. Memorise: drawing hands back when catching a ball (larger Δt, smaller F on hands), jumping on sand vs concrete, crumple zones in cars.

Rapid Revision Cards

Concept → Trap → Example

1) Impulse Formula Chain

Must Memorise

Impulse J = ∫F dt = F_avg × Δt = Δp = m(v − u). All four forms are equal—use whichever is convenient.

  • Impulse is a vector; direction is same as that of the force
  • Dimension: [MLT⁻¹] — same as momentum
  • SI unit: N·s = kg·m/s
  • F-t graph: area under curve = impulse (even if force varies)
Example (NEET-style)A 0.15 kg ball initially moving at 20 m/s is stopped in 0.05 s. Impulse = Δp = 0.15×0 − 0.15×20 = −3 N·s. Average force = 3/0.05 = 60 N.

2) Inverse F-Δt Relationship

Application Focus

For a fixed impulse (fixed Δp), F_avg × Δt = constant. Increasing contact time reduces the average force.

  • Catching a ball: draw hands back to increase Δt → reduce F on hands
  • Jumping on sand not concrete: sand yields, increasing Δt → smaller F (no injury)
  • Crumple zones: car deforms, increasing Δt → reduce impulsive force on passengers
  • Hitting a ball: decrease Δt (wrist flick) → large F → large acceleration
Example (NEET-style)China wares wrapped in straw: straw increases Δt of impact → F on the ware decreases, preventing breakage despite the same impulse delivered by the drop.

US Curriculum Gaps

How the AP Physics treatment compares with NEET scope.

F-t Graph Emphasis in NEET

NEET frequently asks students to compute impulse as the area under a variable force-time graph. AP Physics covers this but NEET MCQs more often use triangular or trapezoidal shapes requiring area calculation.

  • Practise area calculations for triangular, rectangular, and combined F-t graphs.
  • For triangular: J = ½ base × height. For trapezoidal: J = (sum of parallel sides)/2 × height (time width).

Real-Life Application MCQs

NEET tests the F-Δt inverse relationship through scenario MCQs (cricket, diving, car safety). AP Physics covers this conceptually but NEET makes these application questions a regular exam component.

  • Memorise all six standard NEET application scenarios listed in the revision cards.
  • Questions might ask: which action reduces force on the body? — answer is always the one that increases contact time.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
What is the physical significance of impulse?
Impulse represents the total mechanical effect of a force over a time interval. It equals the change in momentum regardless of how the force varies with time. This makes impulse useful precisely when forces are not constant (e.g., collisions, where force peaks unpredictably — we measure the net change in momentum instead).
Can impulse be negative?
Yes. Impulse is a vector. If the force (or the change in momentum Δp) is directed opposite to the chosen positive direction, the impulse is negative. For example, a ball initially moving at +10 m/s stopped by a force gives impulse = m(0 − 10) = −10m N·s (negative, opposing original motion).
When does the impulsive force approximation break down?
The ‘impulsive force’ assumption (very large force, very short time, fixed impulse) breaks down when Δt is not negligible compared to the dynamic time scale of the system (e.g., in slow pushes or pulls). For collisions and impacts where Δt ≲ 0.01 s, the approximation is valid.
Is impulse equal to momentum or change in momentum?
Impulse equals the CHANGE in momentum (Δp = p_final − p_initial), not the total momentum. NEET MCQs sometimes state ‘impulse equals momentum’ as an incorrect option to trap students — always write Δp, not p.
