Motion of Connected Block Over Pulley (Atwood Machine) – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

The Atwood machine is one of the most tested NEET setups: two masses m₁ and m₂ connected by a string over a frictionless, massless pulley. The heavier mass descends, lighter ascends. Key results: acceleration a = (m₂−m₁)g/(m₁+m₂) and tension T = 2m₁m₂g/(m₁+m₂). Extended to 3-mass pulley systems, these formulas remain the anchor for ≥4 NEET questions over every 6-year exam cycle.

Preparation Strategy

1

Derive from FBD — Never Memorise Blindly Draw FBDs for m₁ (going up) and m₂ (going down). For m₁: T − m₁g = m₁a. For m₂: m₂g − T = m₂a. Add the two equations to eliminate T, giving a = (m₂−m₁)g/(m₁+m₂).

2

Memorise T = 2m₁m₂g/(m₁+m₂) This harmonic-mean-like formula gives the tension. Substitute into either FBD equation to verify. Note T = 2m₁m₂g/(m₁+m₂) is always less than the weight of either mass when m₁ ≠ 0.

3

Three-Mass Compound System When a third mass m₃ hangs from m₂ (or from the pulley axle via T₂), write separate FBD equations for each mass. T₁ and T₂ satisfy separate Newton’s 2nd Law equations per block.

Quick Revision Cards

1) Two-Mass Atwood — Core Derivation

Must Know

Two masses m₁ < m₂ connected over frictionless pulley. m₂ descends, m₁ ascends with common acceleration a. FBD for m₂: m₂g − T = m₂a. FBD for m₁: T − m₁g = m₁a. Adding: a = (m₂−m₁)g/(m₁+m₂).

  • a = (m₂−m₁)g / (m₁+m₂)
  • T = 2m₁m₂g / (m₁+m₂)
  • When m₁ = m₂: a = 0, T = m₁g = m₂g (static)
Example (NEET-style)m₁ = 3 kg, m₂ = 5 kg, g = 10 m/s²: a = (2×10)/8 = 2.5 m/s²; T = 2×3×5×10/8 = 37.5 N.

2) Tension in Fixed Support (Pulley Axle)

NEET Trap

The pulley support bears the combined pull of both string segments, each carrying tension T. Total tension on support = T₁ + T₂ = 2T = 4m₁m₂g/(m₁+m₂). This is sometimes confused with T alone.

  • Support tension = 2T (not T)
  • T₂ = 4m₁m₂g / (m₁+m₂)
  • NEET sometimes asks for ‘force exerted on the ceiling’ — this is T₂
Example (NEET-style)m₁ = 3 kg, m₂ = 5 kg: Support = 2 × 37.5 = 75 N. Not 37.5 N.

3) Three-Mass System

Extended Concept

Third mass m₃ hangs from m₂ (via T₂). Now the heavier side is m₂+m₃ combined. Acceleration a = [(m₂+m₃)−m₁]g/(m₁+m₂+m₃). T₁ and T₂ are derived separately from their respective FBD equations.

  • T₁ (main string) = 2m₁(m₂+m₃)g/(m₁+m₂+m₃)
  • T₂ (string between m₂ and m₃) = 2m₁m₃g/(m₁+m₂+m₃)
  • Support tension = 4m₁(m₂+m₃)g/(m₁+m₂+m₃)
Example (NEET-style)Write separate Newton’s equations for m₁ (going up) and for m₂, m₃ (both going down) to get T₁ and T₂.

US Curriculum Bridging

Atwood machine is standard US AP Physics content. Compound pulley extensions and the support tension question are potentially new challenges.

Support (Fixed) Tension

US AP Physics students are often taught to compute T in the string but rarely asked for the tension in the pulley’s support (which is 2T for a fixed pulley). NEET targets this directly.

  • Remember: fixed pulley redirects force but does not reduce it. Each string leg carries T, so support carries 2T.
  • For a movable pulley (different mechanism), force is halved — but the Atwood machine uses a fixed pulley.

Three-Mass Compound Pulley

Textbooks in the US focus the Atwood machine at two masses. The three-mass variant with a string-of-strings configuration is predominantly tested in Indian competitive exams.

  • Draw one FBD per mass; write Newton’s 2nd Law for each; solve simultaneously.
  • Verify: when m₃ = 0, the result should reduce to the standard two-mass Atwood formula.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why is the Atwood machine tension less than either weight?
If m₂ were in free fall, it would have no support and f = m₂g. But it is constrained by the string, which partially holds it back. The tension is less than m₂g (because m₂ is accelerating, not at rest) and greater than m₁g (because m₁ is accelerating upward, more than just supported). Result: m₁g < T < m₂g.
Why does a = (m₂-m₁)g/(m₁+m₂) make intuitive sense?
The net driving force is the weight difference (m₂-m₁)g. The total mass being accelerated is (m₁+m₂). Newton’s 2nd Law directly gives a = net force / total mass. When m₂ = m₁, net force = 0, so a = 0 — the system is in equilibrium.
What is the effect of the pulley’s mass in a real Atwood machine?
A massive pulley has rotational inertia, which reduces the acceleration. This effect is covered in Rotational Motion (Chapter 7), not in the Newton’s Laws treatment. For NEET, the pulley is always assumed massless and frictionless unless stated otherwise.
