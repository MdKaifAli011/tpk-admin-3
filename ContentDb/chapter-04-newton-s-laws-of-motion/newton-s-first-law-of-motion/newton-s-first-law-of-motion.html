Newton's First Law of Motion – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

Newton's First Law states: A body continues in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external force. It gives the qualitative definition of force and establishes the concept of inertia. Mathematically: acceleration = 0 if and only if net force = 0. It was called the Law of Inertia by Galileo. High-yield for NEET conceptual questions on pseudo-force and inertial frames.

Quick Revision Cards

Concept → Trap → Example

1) The Law Statement

Must Know

A body continues to be in its state of rest or of uniform motion along a straight line, unless it is acted upon by some external force to change the state.

  • No net force → no acceleration (velocity remains constant)
  • Also called: Law of Inertia (Galileo named it)
  • Gives qualitative definition of force
Example (NEET-style)A hockey puck sliding on frictionless ice continues indefinitely — no net force acts on it. Add friction and it decelerates due to the friction force.

2) Mathematical Statement

Formula Link

If net force F = 0, then acceleration a = 0, meaning velocity stays constant (which includes being at rest: v = 0, constant).

  • a = 0 ↔ F_net = 0 (biconditional relationship)
  • Body can be at rest (v = 0) or moving (v ≠ 0, constant) when F = 0
  • NEET trap: 'constant velocity' includes zero velocity
Example (NEET-style)A body moving at 10 m/s in deep space (no friction, no gravity) requires zero force to maintain that speed — Newton's 1st Law.

3) Galileo's Law of Inertia

Historical Context

Newton's First Law was originally called the Law of Inertia by Galileo. It corrected Aristotle's fallacy that force is needed to maintain motion.

  • Aristotle (wrong): Force needed to maintain motion
  • Galileo (correct): Force needed only to change motion
  • Newton formalised Galileo's insight into the First Law
Example (NEET-style)Aristotle observed that a cart stops when pushed — but this is due to friction, not because motion requires sustained force. Galileo's inclined plane experiments revealed this error.

Curriculum Bridging — US vs NEET

Key differences between CBSE/NEET and US AP Physics focus areas for Newton's First Law.

Galileo's Precursor and Historical Context

US AP Physics focuses on applying Newton's Laws; NEET also tests the historical lineage (Galileo's Law of Inertia).

  • Know that Newton's 1st Law was called the Law of Inertia by Galileo
  • Aristotle's fallacy (force needed for uniform motion) corrected by Galileo — exam-tested

Qualitative vs Quantitative Force Definition

NEET explicitly tests which law gives the 'qualitative' vs 'quantitative' definition of force.

  • First Law: qualitative — force is what causes change in state of motion
  • Second Law: quantitative — F = ma gives a measurable relationship

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Does Newton's First Law mean a body at rest will always remain at rest?
Only if no net external force acts. If a net force acts, the body will accelerate. Newton's First Law describes the state ONLY in the absence of net force.
Can Newton's First Law be derived from Newton's Second Law?
Yes. If F = 0 in the second law (F = ma), then a = 0, meaning the velocity is constant — which is exactly the statement of the First Law. This is why the Second Law is considered the most fundamental of the three.
What is the difference between Newton's First Law and the Law of Conservation of Momentum?
Newton's First Law applies to a single body in the absence of net force (velocity stays constant). Conservation of momentum applies to a system of bodies — internal forces may act but if net external force is zero, total momentum is conserved. They share the same root condition: zero net external force.
