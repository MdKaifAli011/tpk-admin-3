Frame of Reference – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

A frame of reference is any coordinate system in which an observer makes measurements of position, velocity, and acceleration. Inertial frames (at rest or uniform velocity) obey Newton’s laws directly. Non-inertial frames (accelerating) require a fictitious pseudo force Fₚₛₑᵤᵒ₟ₒ = −ma_frame to make Newton’s laws applicable. This topic is the conceptual bridge between pure Newtonian mechanics and practical problem-solving in accelerating systems such as lifts, trains, and rotating platforms.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Classify Every Frame in the Problem Before applying Newton’s laws, ask: is the observer’s frame accelerating? If yes, add pseudo force = −m × (acceleration of frame) on each object before writing F = ma.

2

Know the Standard Examples Inertial: lift at rest, lift at constant velocity, car at constant speed on straight road. Non-inertial: lift accelerating up or down, car turning, plane taking off, spinning carousel. These appear directly in NEET options.

3

Pseudo Force Direction Pseudo force on an object in a non-inertial frame is OPPOSITE to the acceleration of the frame. If lift accelerates upward at a, pseudo force on person = ma downward. This adds to apparent weight.

Rapid Revision Cards

Concept → Trap → Example

1) Inertial Frame

Definition

A frame of reference that is at rest or moving with uniform (constant) velocity in a straight line. Newton’s laws hold without modification.

  • If net force = 0, acceleration = 0 in inertial frames
  • All frames moving at constant velocity relative to an inertial frame are also inertial
  • Ideally, no perfectly inertial frame exists; Earth is approximately inertial for most problems
Example (NEET-style)A lift moving upward at 5 m/s (constant): inertial frame. A ball released inside behaves normally. A car travelling at 60 km/h on a straight road: inertial frame.

2) Non-Inertial Frame and Pseudo Force

Key Formula

An accelerating frame of reference where Newton’s laws appear violated unless a pseudo force F = −ma_frame is added to each object.

  • Pseudo force = −m × (acceleration of the frame)
  • Direction: opposite to the acceleration of the frame
  • Pseudo force has no physical source; it is a mathematical correction
Example (NEET-style)Lift accelerating upward at 3 m/s²: pseudo force on a 60 kg person = −60 × 3 = −180 N downward. Apparent weight = 60(9.8+3) = 768 N instead of 588 N.

US Curriculum Gaps

Differences between AP Physics and NEET treatment of frames.

Pseudo Force Not Explicitly Named in AP Physics 1

AP Physics 1 avoids pseudo forces in non-inertial frames; instead always analyses from an external inertial frame. NEET problems set inside the accelerating frame require pseudo force to solve directly.

  • Practice both approaches: from outside (inertial frame, apply Newton’s 2nd law normally) and from inside (non-inertial frame, add pseudo force).
  • Both give numerically identical answers; pseudo force method is faster in NEET single-step MCQs.

Earth’s Rotational Non-Inertia

NEET acknowledges Earth is non-inertial due to its rotation but considers it approximately inertial for most lab problems. AP Physics occasionally covers Coriolis effects at advanced level.

  • For NEET: treat Earth as inertial unless the problem explicitly involves the Coriolis or centrifugal effect.
  • NEET may test: sun is a better inertial frame than Earth for planetary motion problems.

An inertial frame is at rest or in uniform straight-line motion. A train at constant velocity on a straight track satisfies this. A car increasing speed is accelerating (non-inertial). A lift with increasing speed is accelerating. A merry-go-round is rotating (centripetal acceleration present — non-inertial).

In the non-inertial train frame, pseudo force = −ma (backward). The pendulum bob experiences pseudo force backward and gravity downward. Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
What is the difference between pseudo force and a real force?
A real force has a physical source (gravitational, electromagnetic, contact) and obeys Newton’s third law (has a reaction pair on another body). A pseudo force has no physical source — it is a mathematical term added when using a non-inertial frame to preserve the form of Newton’s second law. It has no reaction pair.
Is the Coriolis force a pseudo force?
Yes. The Coriolis force arises when analysing motion from a rotating (non-inertial) frame. It is a pseudo force with no reaction partner. In the rotating Earth frame, the Coriolis force deflects moving objects (e.g., winds, projectiles) to the right in the northern hemisphere.
Can we always avoid using pseudo forces by choosing an inertial frame?
Yes. Any problem solvable in a non-inertial frame with pseudo force can also be solved from an inertial (external) frame using only real forces. The two approaches give numerically identical answers. Pseudo forces are a mathematical convenience, not a physical necessity.
Why does the textbook say Earth is ‘approximately’ inertial?
Earth rotates about its axis (centripetal acceleration ≈ 0.034 m/s² at equator) and revolves around the Sun. For most laboratory-scale experiments, these accelerations are negligible compared to g ≈ 9.8 m/s², so Earth is treated as inertial. For very precise experiments (Foucault pendulum, long-range ballistics, planetary motion), Earth’s non-inertial nature must be accounted for.
