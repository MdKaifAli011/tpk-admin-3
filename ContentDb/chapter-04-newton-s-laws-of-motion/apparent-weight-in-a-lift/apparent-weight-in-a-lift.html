Apparent Weight in a Lift – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

Apparent weight is the reading of a weighing machine that supports a body. When the lift accelerates upward, apparent weight R = m(g + a) > mg; when it accelerates downward at a, R = m(g − a); in free fall (a = g downward), R = 0 (weightlessness). This topic is one of the most consistently tested application problems in NEET’s Newton’s Laws questions.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Memorise the 7-Case Table NCERT Table 4.2 has 7 conditions: (1) rest → R = mg; (2) constant velocity (up or down) → R = mg; (3) upward acceleration a → R = m(g+a); (4) a = g upward → R = 2mg; (5) downward acceleration a → R = m(g−a); (6) a = g downward (free fall) → R = 0; (7) a > g downward → R is negative (physically impossible without seat belt). Memorise ALL 7.

2

Fix Sign Convention via FBD Always draw the free body diagram taking upward as positive. Net force = ma = R − mg. Therefore R = m(g + a) for upward a (a is positive), and R = m(g − a) for downward a (a substituted as positive magnitude).

3

Connect to Weightlessness When R = 0, object is in a state of weightlessness but its actual mass and weight (mg) are unchanged. This is the same condition astronauts experience in orbit. NEET MCQs test the distinction between 'weightlessness' and 'zero gravity'.

1) General Formula and Sign Rule

Must Memorise

R = m(g + a_eff) where a_eff = +a if lift accelerates upward, a_eff = −a if accelerates downward. Take upward positive; net force = R − mg = m×(net a). So R = mg + ma = m(g+a) for upward acceleration.

  • Lift at rest or uniform velocity: R = mg (no net acceleration)
  • Lift accelerates up at a: R = m(g+a) > mg (feels heavier)
  • Lift accelerates down at a: R = m(g−a) < mg (feels lighter)
  • Free fall (cable snaps): a = g downward, R = m(g−g) = 0 (weightlessness)
  • Deceleration while moving up = acceleration downward: R = m(g−a)
Example (NEET-style)A 60 kg person stands on a scale in a lift accelerating downward at 2 m/s². g = 10 m/s². R = 60(10−2) = 60×8 = 480 N. Scale reads 48 kgf (less than actual 60 kgf).

2) All 7 Cases Summary

NCERT Table 4.2

Complete 7-case table to memorise for NEET.

  • Case 1: Lift at rest → R = mg
  • Case 2: Constant velocity (any direction) → R = mg
  • Case 3: Accelerating upward at a → R = m(g+a)
  • Case 4: Accelerating upward at a = g → R = 2mg
  • Case 5: Accelerating downward at a → R = m(g−a)
  • Case 6: Accelerating downward at a = g (free fall) → R = 0
  • Case 7: Accelerating downward at a > g → R is negative (not physically possible in free body; implies body leaves scale)
Example (NEET-style)NEET Trap: A lift decelerating while moving upward has the same formula as accelerating downward: R = m(g−a). Both give reduced apparent weight. Deceleration while moving up is equivalent to acceleration directed downward.

US Curriculum Gaps

Differences between the AP Physics and NEET approach to elevator problems.

7-Case Table Format in NEET

NEET uses the NCERT Table 4.2 structure explicitly in questions: students are expected to have memorised all 7 cases. AP Physics derives results case-by-case from Newton’s Second Law applied to FBDs, which is correct but slower in an MCQ format.

  • Memorise all 7 cases by number so you can identify the correct formula instantly in 30 seconds.
  • For AP-background students: your FBD derivation skill is an asset — use it to derive any case you forget.

Weightlessness Conceptual Distinction

NEET specifically tests the definition: weightlessness = R = 0 (scale reads zero) even though actual weight mg still acts. AP Physics covers this but NEET MCQs explicitly distinguish weightlessness from zero gravity, which does not exist at Earth’s surface.

  • Weightlessness: astronauts in orbit, free-falling lift — R = 0 but actual weight mg is non-zero.
  • Zero gravity: only in deep space far from all masses. Not applicable near Earth’s surface.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
What is actual weight vs apparent weight?
Actual weight is the gravitational force W = mg. It is always the same for a given mass at a given location. Apparent weight (R) is the force exerted by the supporting surface (scale reading). R can be more, less, or equal to W depending on the acceleration of the lift.
Is weightlessness the same as being in zero gravity?
No. Weightlessness means R = 0 (the surface exerts no normal force), which happens in free fall or orbit. Actual gravity g still acts on the person. Zero gravity strictly means g ≈ 0, which only occurs in deep space far from all massive bodies.
What happens when a > g downward?
Mathematically R = m(g − a) becomes negative, which is physically impossible (a surface can only push, not pull). In reality the person leaves the scale surface and only gravity acts on them (free fall with the lift acting on them differently).
Why is apparent weight unchanged at constant velocity?
At constant velocity, net acceleration = 0. Newton’s second law gives R − mg = 0, so R = mg. The speed of the lift (whether zero or 10 m/s upward or 5 m/s downward) does not matter; only acceleration determines the apparent weight.
