Equilibrium of Concurrent Forces – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

Concurrent forces all act at the same point. A body under concurrent forces is in equilibrium when the vector sum of all forces is zero: ΣF = 0 (i.e. ΣFx = 0 and ΣFy = 0). Three concurrent forces in equilibrium can be represented by the three sides of a triangle taken in order. Lami's Theorem provides an elegant ratio-based solution: F₁/sinα = F₂/sinβ = F₃/sinγ. High-yield for NEET inclined plane, hanging mass, and FBD problems.

1) Concurrent Forces — Definition

Must Know

If all the forces working on a body are acting on the same point, they are said to be concurrent. A body under concurrent forces is in equilibrium when said state (rest/uniform motion) does not change.

  • Concurrent = all force lines of action pass through one point
  • Equilibrium: no change in state of rest or uniform motion
  • Necessary condition: vector sum of all forces = 0
Example (NEET-style)A traffic light hanging from two cables and gravity forms three concurrent forces at the junction point. In static equilibrium, the three forces balance to ΣF = 0.

2) Equilibrium Conditions

Core Formula

For equilibrium: ΣF_net = 0, which means ΣFx = 0 and ΣFy = 0 (and ΣFz = 0 in 3D). These are the necessary and sufficient conditions for concurrent force equilibrium.

  • Resolve all forces into x and y components
  • Set net force in each direction to zero
  • Solve the resulting equations for unknown forces or angles
Example (NEET-style)A block on an inclined plane held by a horizontal string: resolve W (weight), N (normal), T (tension) along and perpendicular to plane. Set ΣF‖ = 0 and ΣF⊥ = 0 to find T and N.

3) Triangle Law for Three Forces

Geometric Method

Three concurrent forces in equilibrium can be represented completely by the three sides of a triangle taken in order. The triangle closes (no resultant) — reflecting ΣF = 0.

  • Draw the forces head-to-tail as triangle sides
  • If the triangle closes, the forces are in equilibrium
  • This is a geometric equivalent of the vector sum = 0 condition
Example (NEET-style)Forces of 3 N, 4 N, and 5 N can form a 3-4-5 right triangle. If oriented correctly head-to-tail, they are in equilibrium.

4) Lami's Theorem

NEET Favourite

For three concurrent coplanar forces in equilibrium: F₁/sin α = F₂/sin β = F₃/sin γ, where α, β, γ are the angles between the OTHER two forces (not from the axes).

  • α = angle between F₂ and F₃; β = between F₃ and F₁; γ = between F₁ and F₂
  • α + β + γ = 360°
  • Use when you know angles between forces — faster than resolving components
Example (NEET-style)A mass hangs from two strings making 30° and 60° with the vertical. Using Lami's Theorem on the junction point (three forces: T₁, T₂, and weight W) gives T₁ and T₂ directly without resolving components.

Curriculum Bridging — US vs NEET

Key differences in equilibrium treatment between NEET and US AP Physics.

Lami's Theorem

Not part of US AP Physics curriculum. NEET tests it directly with numerical MCQs.

  • Memorise the formula: F₁/sinα = F₂/sinβ = F₃/sinγ
  • Crucial: angles α, β, γ are between force pairs, not with coordinate axes
  • Faster than component resolution for three-force problems

Triangle Law for Forces

US AP uses the polygon method generally; NEET explicitly tests the triangle rule as a named theorem for three concurrent equilibrium forces.

  • Three concurrent equilibrium forces form a closed triangle
  • Diagram MCQs test whether a set of forces (given as triangle sides) is in equilibrium

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
What is the difference between equilibrium of concurrent forces and general equilibrium?
General equilibrium requires both ΣF = 0 (translational equilibrium) AND Στ = 0 (rotational equilibrium, torques about any axis). For concurrent forces (all acting at one point), torques about the point of concurrency are automatically zero — so only ΣF = 0 is needed. Concurrent force equilibrium is a simpler special case.
When should I use Lami's Theorem vs. component resolution?
Use Lami's Theorem when exactly three concurrent forces act and you know the angles between the forces (or need to find them). It gives the answer in one step using the sine rule. Use component resolution (ΣFx = 0, ΣFy = 0) when forces are not all known, when more than three forces act, or when the geometry is more easily expressed in Cartesian coordinates.
Can a body in equilibrium be moving?
Yes. Translational equilibrium means zero net force → zero acceleration → constant velocity. A body in uniform motion (constant speed in a straight line) satisfies ΣF = 0 and is in dynamic equilibrium. Static equilibrium is the special case where velocity = 0 as well.
