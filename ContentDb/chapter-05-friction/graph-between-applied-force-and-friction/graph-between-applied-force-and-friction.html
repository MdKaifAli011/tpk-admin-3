Graph Between Applied Force and Friction – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

The friction vs applied force graph (Fig. 5.2 in NCERT) shows three distinct regions: OA (static friction, self-adjusting, rises linearly with applied force), point A (limiting friction, peak), and BC (kinetic friction, constant and less than limiting). The key visual message is that kinetic friction is less than maximum static friction and is independent of applied force. NEET directly presents this graph and asks analytical questions about each region.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Memorise the Four Segments OA: static friction (Fs = applied force, linear rise). Point A: limiting friction (peak, Fs = Fl = μs R). A to B: brief drop as motion begins (transition from static to kinetic). BC: kinetic friction (constant, Fk = μk R, parallel to x-axis).

2

Emphasise BC is Horizontal Kinetic friction does NOT change with applied force — segment BC is parallel to the x-axis. This is the most tested feature. Contrast with OA which has slope = 1 (friction equals applied force in the linear static region).

3

Link Graph to Coefficient Inequalities The graph visually confirms Fk < Fl (μk < μs). The drop from A (peak) to BC (plateau) is the graphical representation of this inequality. NEET MCQs connect the graph to the coefficient relationship.

1) Four Regions of the Friction Graph

Must Memorise

OA: static friction = applied force (slope = 1, linear). Point A: limiting friction Fl = μs R (peak value). A-to-B: friction drops briefly as motion begins. BC: kinetic friction Fk = μk R (constant, parallel to x-axis). Fk < Fl always.

  • Region OA: Fs equals applied force P; object at rest; self-adjusting
  • Point A: maximum static friction, Fl = μs R; motion about to begin
  • A → B: slight drop as static friction hand-off to kinetic; irregularities unlock
  • Region BC: Fk = constant = μk R; parallel to x-axis regardless of applied force
  • Gradient of OA = 1 (friction tracks applied force exactly in static regime)
Example (NEET-style)If μs = 0.5 and the block weighs 100 N, then A is at F = 50 N. Once the block starts moving (μk = 0.4), kinetic friction = 40 N along BC, regardless of whether applied force is 50 N or 200 N.

2) Key Features to Identify on the Graph

Graph Reading

NEET questions present the graph and ask: what does segment OA represent? What is the physical significance of point A? Why is segment BC parallel to x-axis? What does the drop A→B represent?

  • OA represents static friction (self-adjusting, not yet limiting)
  • A = limiting friction (peak, first onset of motion)
  • A→B drop = friction decreases as motion starts (kinetic < static)
  • BC = kinetic friction (constant, motion-dependent but speed-independent)
  • BC parallel to x-axis = kinetic friction does NOT depend on applied force
Example (NEET-style)MCQ: ‘In the graph, segment BC is parallel to the applied force axis. This shows that kinetic friction is —’ Answer: independent of applied force (constant once motion starts).

US Curriculum Gaps

AP Physics vs NEET on the friction vs applied force graph.

Segment A→B Transition

AP Physics introduces the static-kinetic transition but does not emphasise the brief drop (A→B) between maximum static and kinetic friction as a testable graph feature. NEET includes this drop and asks why it occurs.

  • The A→B drop occurs because as motion starts, surface irregularities no longer have time to fully interlock.
  • NEET MCQ: ‘On the friction-applied force graph, segment AB shows a brief decrease. This indicates that —’ Answer: kinetic friction is less than limiting friction.

Graphical Proof of μk < μs

NEET uses the graph as a visual proof of the coefficient inequality. The y-coordinate at A > y-coordinate along BC visually confirms Fl > Fk, hence μs > μk. This graphical-to-algebraic connection is tested directly.

  • y at A = Fl = μs R; y along BC = Fk = μk R. Since A is higher than BC, μs > μk.
  • This graphical observation is a NEET-style ‘what can be concluded from this graph’ question.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
What is the physical meaning of the slope of segment OA being exactly 1?
In the static regime (OA), static friction self-adjusts to exactly equal the applied force. So as applied force increases by 1 N, static friction increases by exactly 1 N — slope = 1. This continues until the applied force reaches Fl (point A). Beyond A, the body begins to move and the self-adjusting mechanism breaks down.
Why does friction drop from A to B when motion starts?
In the static state, surface irregularities have fully interlocked over the contact time. When motion begins, these interlocked irregularities disengage and one surface skims over the other at speed — the real contact area (and bonding time) decreases. This creates a lower kinetic friction. The drop from A to B represents this decrease from μs R to μk R.
If applied force exceeds point A, can friction increase again?
No. Once motion has started, friction stays at the kinetic value Fk = μk R (segment BC), which is constant. Applying more force increases the body’s acceleration but does NOT increase the friction force. Fk depends only on μk and R, not on the applied force.
Is the friction-applied force graph the same for all surfaces?
The shape is qualitatively the same (OA rising, peak at A, drop to BC plateau), but the y-coordinates depend on the specific μs and μk values of the surface pair. Smoother surfaces have lower A-peaks; rougher surfaces have higher peaks. The ratio Fl/Fk = μs/μk is typically in the range 1.1–1.5 for common materials.
