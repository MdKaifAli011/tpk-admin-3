Motion of Two Bodies One on Other – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

When body A (mass m) rests on body B (mass M), and a force F is applied to either body, four distinct cases arise depending on whether friction is absent, below limiting value, or above limiting value. When F < μs mg (limiting friction between A and B), both bodies move together with common acceleration F/(M+m). When F > μs mg, bodies move separately with kinetic friction coupling them. A further complication arises when there is also friction between B and the floor. NEET tests all four cases across both force configurations (force on upper body A and force on lower body B).

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Master the Two Combined-Motion Conditions Force on upper body A: combined motion if F < μs mg. Force on lower body B: combined motion if F < μs(m+M)g. Both conditions must be instant recalls. The limiting friction between A and B (= μs mg) governs the A-on-top case; the limiting friction between B and floor governs the B-case threshold.

2

Draw Separate FBDs for A and B For F > limiting: draw FBD for A and B separately. Friction between A and B (= μk mg) is the only horizontal force on A (in A-on-top case); for B it appears as a force in the direction of F. This gives aA = μk g and aB = (F − μk mg)/M.

3

Check Status of B-Floor Friction Before Solving If the problem mentions friction between B and floor (μ'), first check if B moves at all: B moves only if kinetic friction coupling from A exceeds B-floor limiting friction. If B stays, static friction on B-floor adjusts to match. Never assume B moves.

1) Force on Upper Body A — Four Cases

Must Memorise

Case (i): No friction. aA = F/m; aB = 0. Case (ii): Friction A-B only, F < Fl. Both move together: a = F/(M+m). Case (iii): Friction A-B only, F > Fl. Bodies separate: aA = (F − μk mg)/m; aB = μk mg/M. Case (iv): Friction on B-floor too. B moves only if kinetic friction from A on B exceeds μ'(M+m)g.

  • Combined motion condition (force on A): F < μs mg
  • Combined acceleration: a = F/(M+m)
  • Separate: aA = (F − μk mg)/m; aB = μk mg/M
  • Friction on A when F > Fl: backwards (opposing relative motion of A on B)
  • Friction on B from A: forwards (in direction A tends to move relative to B)
Example (NEET-style)m = 2 kg, M = 3 kg, μs = 0.4, μk = 0.3, g = 10 m/s². Fl = μs mg = 8 N. If F = 6 N < 8 N: a = 6/5 = 1.2 m/s² (both together). If F = 15 N > 8 N: aA = (15−6)/(2) = 4.5 m/s²; aB = 6/3 = 2 m/s².

2) Force on Lower Body B — Four Cases

High-Yield

Case (i): No friction. aB = F/M; aA = 0 (remains at rest relative to ground). Case (ii): Friction A-B only, F < μs(M+m)g. Both move together: a = F/(M+m). Case (iii): Friction A-B only, F > μs mg. Bodies separate: aA = μk g; aB = (F − μk mg)/M. Case (iv): Friction on B-floor too: B moves only if F > μk(m+M)g.

  • Combined motion condition (force on B): F < μs(M+m)g
  • Combined acceleration: a = F/(M+m) (same formula as force-on-A case)
  • Separate (F > Fl): aA = μk g; aB = (F − μk mg)/M
  • When B is accelerated rapidly under A, A lags behind — friction from B pulls A forward
  • Pseudo-force on A (in B’s frame) = ma = mF/(M+m) < μs mg for combined motion
Example (NEET-style)m = 2 kg, M = 5 kg, μs = 0.3, μk = 0.25, g = 10 m/s². Combined condition: F < 0.3×2×10 = 6 N? No: threshold = μs(m+M)g = 0.3×7×10 = 21 N. If F = 15 N: both move together, a = 15/7 ≈ 2.14 m/s². If F = 30 N: aA = μk g = 2.5 m/s²; aB = (30−5)/(5) = 5 m/s².

How AP Physics and US curricula compare to NEET scope on stacked-body friction problems.

Systematic Case Analysis Not Required in AP Physics

AP Physics tests one or two friction setups in a single problem. NEET requires knowing 8 sub-cases (4 for force on A, 4 for force on B) and determining which case applies from given data. Students from US curricula should practise categorising the sub-case before computing accelerations.

  • Identify friction condition: none / F < Fl / F > Fl / floor friction present.
  • Apply combined or separate motion equations for each sub-case.

Fall-Time Formula When A Slides Off B

When F > Fl (A slides on B), A will eventually fall off B after time t = √(2L/a_rel), where L = length of B and a_rel = aA − aB is relative acceleration of A w.r.t. B. This formula is NEET-specific; AP Physics does not test it.

  • Relative acceleration a_rel = aA − aB when force is on A.
  • Fall time t = √(2L/a_rel) derived from s = ½at².

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why does the combined motion threshold differ for force on A vs. force on B?
When force F acts on A (upper body), the only friction that can accelerate B is the friction between A and B. This friction ≤ μs mg. So for B to keep up with A: μs mg must be enough to give B the same acceleration, which requires F < μs mg total (not just for B). When force F acts on B, the friction between A and B must accelerate A: required friction = mF/(M+m). For combined motion: mF/(M+m) < μs mg ⇒ F < μs(M+m)g.
What is friction force on B when F > limiting (force applied to A, no floor friction)?
When A slides on B (F > Fl), kinetic friction between A and B = μk mg acts backward on A (opposing A's forward sliding) and forward on B (Newton's third law pair). So friction on B = μk mg in the direction of applied force F. This accelerates B: aB = μk mg/M.
If there is friction between B and the floor, when does B remain stationary?
B is accelerated by friction from A, which at most equals μk mg (kinetic friction between A and B). B stays stationary if this μk mg ≤ μ'(M+m)g (limiting friction between B and floor). When B is stationary, static friction from floor adjusts to balance exactly the force transmitted to B. B moves only if μk mg > μ'(M+m)g.
Can the block A ever slide backward off B?
Yes — when force is applied to B (lower body) and F > μs mg, B accelerates more than A, so relative to B, A moves backward and eventually falls off the rear end of B. Fall time = √(2L/a_rel) where a_rel = aB − aA = (F − μk mg)/M − μk g is the relative retardation of A w.r.t. B.
