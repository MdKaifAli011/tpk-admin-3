Calculation of Required Force – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

To move a block on a rough surface, the required force depends on the angle of application. Six scenarios — pull/push at angle α on horizontal or inclined planes — each give a different force formula. The critical result: applying force at angle α = tan⁻¹(μ) (the angle of friction) gives the minimum required force P_min = μmg/√(1+μ²). NEET tests both scenario-specific formulas and the minimum-force optimisation. Understanding which angle minimises P is a high-frequency NEET topic.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Draw FBD for Each Scenario For each of the 6 scenarios (pull/push on horizontal; pull up/push down incline; etc.), draw the free body diagram. Identify horizontal equilibrium (P cosα = μR) and vertical equilibrium (R = mg ± P sinα). This prevents the #1 error of applying horizontal friction formula to angled force problems.

2

Derive P_min by Differentiation P ≥ μmg / (cosα + μ sinα). Differentiate denominator: d(cosα + μ sinα)/dα = 0 ⇒ μ = cotα = 1/tanα ⇒ tanα = μ ⇒ α = tan⁻¹(μ) = angle of friction. Substitute back: P_min = μmg/√(1+μ²).

3

Memorise the Minimum Force Result P_min = μmg/√(1+μ²) and optimal angle α = tan⁻¹(μ). This is the single most tested result. Note: P_min < μmg always (pulling at angle is always more efficient than horizontal pull on level surface).

1) Six Force Scenarios

Must Memorise

Six classic cases arise by varying the direction (pull/push), surface (horizontal/inclined), and direction of motion (up/down incline). In each case, resolve the applied force P into components parallel and perpendicular to the contact surface, update R (which changes if P has a vertical component), then apply F = μR to find the friction term.

  • Horizontal pull at angle α above horizontal: R = mg − P sinα; P cosα = μR
  • Horizontal push at angle α below horizontal: R = mg + P sinα; P cosα = μR
  • Pull up incline at angle α from plane: R = W cosλ − P sinα; P = W sin(θ+λ)/cos(α−θ)
  • Push down incline at angle α from plane: use corresponding equilibrium equations
  • In ALL cases: update R first, then apply F = μR to get correct friction
  • P_min is achieved when α = tan⁻¹(μ) = angle of friction
Example (NEET-style)Pull at angle α on horizontal surface (mass 5 kg, μ = 0.4, g = 10). R = 50 − P sinα. P cosα = 0.4(50 − P sinα). At α = tan⁻¹(0.4) ≈ 21.8°: P_min = 0.4×50/√(1+0.16) = 20/1.077 ≈ 18.6 N.

2) Minimum Force Formula

Formula Card

P_min = μmg/√(1+μ²), achieved by pulling at angle α = tan⁻¹(μ) = angle of friction. The physical insight: pulling upward reduces normal reaction (hence friction) but also has less horizontal component — the optimal balance is exactly at the angle of friction. P_min is always less than the horizontal force needed (μmg).

  • P_min = μmg/√(1+μ²) — minimum force to move block on horizontal surface
  • Optimal angle α_opt = tan⁻¹(μ) = angle of friction
  • sinα_opt = μ/√(1+μ²); cosα_opt = 1/√(1+μ²)
  • P_min < μmg always (angular pull is more efficient than horizontal pull)
  • P_min is obtained by maximising denominator cosα + μ sinα
Example (NEET-style)m = 10 kg, μ = 0.3, g = 10 m/s². P_horizontal = μmg = 30 N. P_min = 0.3×100/√(1+0.09) = 30/1.044 ≈ 28.7 N. Optimal angle = tan⁻¹(0.3) ≈ 16.7°. Pulling at 16.7° needs 1.3 N less force than pulling horizontally.

US Curriculum Gaps

How AP Physics and common US curricula compare to NEET scope on required-force calculation.

Minimum-Force Optimisation Not Tested in AP Physics

AP Physics tests basic friction on horizontal surfaces but does not require derivation of the minimum force or the optimal angle. NEET explicitly tests P_min = μmg/√(1+μ²) as a formula recall and as a derivation exercise, including the role of calculus in finding the optimum angle.

  • Derive P_min by maximising (cosα + μ sinα) with respect to α.
  • Know that optimal angle = angle of friction = tan⁻¹(μ).

Inclined Plane Force Formulas Vary by Scenario

US curricula typically solve only one or two inclined-plane friction configurations. NEET requires all four inclined-plane cases (pull up, push up, pull down, push down) with correct sign conventions and friction direction. Each has a distinct formula derived from equilibrium along and perpendicular to the incline.

  • Always identify direction of impending motion before assigning friction direction.
  • Perpendicular equilibrium always gives R first, then use F = μR.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why does pulling at an angle reduce the required force compared to a horizontal pull?
When force P is applied at angle α above horizontal, its upward component P sinα reduces the normal reaction: R = mg − P sinα. Reduced R means reduced friction F = μR. However, the horizontal component P cosα also decreases with increasing α. The optimal trade-off occurs at α = angle of friction, giving P_min = μmg/√(1+μ²) < μmg.
Why is pushing harder than pulling (for the same angle)?
For a push at angle α below horizontal, the vertical component P sinα acts downward, increasing normal reaction: R = mg + P sinα. Greater R means greater friction F = μR. So pushing always requires more force than pulling at the same angle. Minimum push force is derived using similar calculus but the result is greater than P_min for pulling.
Is the minimum-force formula P_min = μmg/√(1+μ²) valid on inclined planes?
No — P_min = μmg/√(1+μ²) applies only when pulling a block on a horizontal surface to initiate motion. On inclined planes, the formula changes because both the weight component along the plane and the normal reaction depend on the plane angle λ. The correct approach is to resolve forces along and perpendicular to the inclined surface for each specific configuration.
What physical quantity equals tan⁻¹(μ)?
tan⁻¹(μ) equals the angle of friction θ: it is the angle that the resultant of limiting friction and normal reaction makes with the normal. The same angle appears as the optimal pull angle for minimum force. This unifies two apparently separate concepts in the Friction chapter.
