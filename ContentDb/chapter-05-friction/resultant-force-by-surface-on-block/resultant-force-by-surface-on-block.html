Resultant Force by Surface on Block – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

When a block rests on a surface, the surface exerts two perpendicular force components on it: the normal reaction R (perpendicular to surface) and the friction force F (parallel to surface). The resultant contact force S = √(F² + R²). At zero friction (smooth surface) S = mg (minimum). At limiting friction S = mg√(μ²+1) (maximum). NEET tests the range mg ≤ S ≤ mg√(μ²+1) and the vector nature of the contact force from surfaces.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Derive S from Pythagoras S = √(F² + R²). Substitute F = μR = μmg and R = mg: S = √((μmg)²+(mg)²) = mg√(μ²+1). This derivation is the only one needed for NEET.

2

Memorise the Range When μ = 0: S = mg (minimum). At limiting friction: S = mg√(μ²+1) (maximum). Range: mg ≤ S ≤ mg√(μ²+1). NEET may ask for either extreme.

3

Distinguish S from R and F R = mg (normal reaction, vertical for horizontal surface). F ≤ μmg (friction, horizontal). S = vector sum of R and F. Never write S = R + F (scalar addition is wrong for perpendicular vectors).

1) Net Contact Force Formula

Must Memorise

The surface exerts a normal reaction R perpendicular to itself and a friction force F parallel to itself. Their vector resultant is S = √(F² + R²). For a block on a horizontal surface, R = mg and at limiting friction F = μmg, giving S = mg√(μ²+1). The range is mg ≤ S ≤ mg√(μ²+1).

  • S = √(F² + R²) — Pythagorean magnitude of contact force
  • Minimum S = mg when μ = 0 (frictionless surface)
  • Maximum S = mg√(μ²+1) at limiting friction
  • S is always ≥ mg for any surface with friction
  • Angle of S with normal = angle of friction θ = tan⁻¹(μ)
Example (NEET-style)Block of mass 2 kg, μ = 0.75, g = 10 m/s². At limiting friction: S = 2×10×√(0.75²+1) = 20√(0.5625+1) = 20√1.5625 = 20×1.25 = 25 N. Minimum S (smooth surface) = 2×10 = 20 N.

2) Range of Contact Force

Exam Trap

The contact force S cannot be less than mg (the weight supporting component) because the normal reaction alone equals mg when there is no friction. As friction increases from 0 to its limiting value μmg, S increases from mg to mg√(μ²+1). This range is a direct NEET conceptual MCQ.

  • S is minimum for smooth surface: S_min = mg
  • S is maximum at limiting friction: S_max = mg√(μ²+1)
  • For rough surface in static equilibrium: mg ≤ S ≤ mg√(μ²+1)
  • The resultant S always makes angle θ ≤ angle of friction with normal
  • NEET trap: S ≠ mg + μmg (scalar sum); must use Pythagoras
Example (NEET-style)If μ = 0.6, g = 10 m/s², m = 5 kg: S_min = 50 N; S_max = 5×10×√(0.36+1) = 50√1.36 ≈ 50×1.166 ≈ 58.3 N. The range is 50 N ≤ S ≤ 58.3 N.

US Curriculum Gaps

How AP Physics and common US curricula compare to NEET scope on resultant contact force.

Resultant Contact Force Range Not Tested in AP Physics

AP Physics 1 and C treat normal force and friction force as separate vectors; the concept of a 'resultant contact force S' with a specific range formula is not tested. NEET Class 11 explicitly requires the range mg ≤ S ≤ mg√(μ²+1) to be memorised and applied in MCQs.

  • Learn S = √(N² + f²) as the contact force resultant.
  • Memorise both extreme values: S_min = mg and S_max = mg√(μ²+1).

Angle of Resultant with Normal

The resultant contact force S makes an angle θ = tan⁻¹(f/N) with the normal. This angle equals the angle of friction at limiting friction, and is less than the angle of friction when friction is below its limit. AP Physics does not emphasise this angular relationship.

  • At limiting friction: angle of S with normal = angle of friction = tan⁻¹(μ).
  • This geometric link between S and angle of friction is an NEET-targeted concept.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why is the minimum contact force equal to mg and not zero?
The block is in vertical equilibrium on the surface. The surface must support the block's weight, so the normal reaction R = mg is always present. Even with zero friction (smooth surface), the surface still pushes up with force mg. Hence S = √(0² + mg²) = mg is the minimum. S = 0 would mean the block is floating, which contradicts contact.
What is the difference between the contact force S and the normal reaction R?
The normal reaction R is only the component of the contact force perpendicular to the surface. The contact force S = √(R² + F²) is the total (vector sum of R and friction F). For a vertical smooth surface, R can equal S because friction is zero in that direction. For rough horizontal surfaces, S > R always when friction is present.
At what condition does S equal mg√(μ²+1)?
S = mg√(μ²+1) when friction acts at its limiting (maximum) value F = μmg. This is when the block is on the verge of sliding. Below limiting friction, F < μmg and hence S is between mg and mg√(μ²+1).
Does the contact force depend on the applied external force?
Yes. As an applied force is increased from zero to limiting friction, the friction component F increases (self-adjusting), so S = √(F² + R²) increases from mg to mg√(μ²+1). Once motion starts Fk = μk R is fixed, so S stabilises at mg√(μk²+1). The contact force is not constant unless friction has reached its limit.
