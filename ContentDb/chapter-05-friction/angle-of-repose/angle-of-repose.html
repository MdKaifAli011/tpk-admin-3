Angle of Repose – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

The angle of repose (α) is the maximum angle of an inclined plane with the horizontal such that a body placed on it just begins to slide. Resolving forces along and perpendicular to the incline: μs = tan α. Since angle of friction also gives tan θ = μs, the two angles are equal: α = θ. NEET tests the definition, the derivation μs = tan α, and the equality with angle of friction.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Derive μs = tan α from Inclined Plane FBD Block of mass m on incline at angle α. At the point of just beginning to slide: mg sin α = F_l (limiting friction up the incline) and R = mg cos α (normal reaction). Since F_l = μs R: mg sin α = μs mg cos α. Dividing: tan α = μs.

2

Prove α = θ (Angle of Repose = Angle of Friction) From angle of friction: tan θ = μs. From angle of repose: tan α = μs. Since both equal μs, we have α = θ. This equality is explicitly tested in NEET: ‘what is the relation between angle of friction and angle of repose?’

3

Numerical Applications Given μs, compute angle of repose: α = tan⁻¹(μs). Given angle α, compute μs = tan α. These are 1-step numerical MCQs. Know exact values: tan 30° = 1/√3 ≈ 0.577; tan 45° = 1; tan 60° = √3 ≈ 1.73.

1) Angle of Repose: Definition and Derivation

Must Memorise

Angle of repose (α) is the maximum angle of inclined plane with horizontal such that a body placed on it just begins to slide. FBD derivation: mg sin α = μs mg cos α ⇒ tan α = μs. Hence the coefficient of limiting friction equals the tangent of angle of repose.

  • Definition: angle at which body ‘just begins to slide’ (onset of motion, not during motion)
  • Use limiting (maximum static) friction in derivation, not kinetic friction
  • tan α = μs (same formula as angle of friction tan θ = μs)
  • Therefore: α = θ (angle of repose equals angle of friction)
  • Greater coefficient μs → larger angle of repose (steeper slope before sliding)
Example (NEET-style)μs = 0.5 ⇒ angle of repose = tan⁻¹(0.5) ≈ 26.6°. A block on a 30° slope with μs = 0.577 will just begin to slide at 30° (since tan 30° = 0.577 = μs).

2) Relation to Angle of Friction: α = θ

Key Relation

Angle of repose α = angle of friction θ, because both obey tan = μs. This equality is the bridge between two different physical setups (inclined plane vs flat surface) and appears as a direct NEET MCQ comparison question.

  • Angle of friction θ: measured from normal reaction in flat-surface resultant vector triangle
  • Angle of repose α: measured from horizontal in inclined plane geometry
  • Both give tan = μs ⇒ α = θ (numerically equal)
  • NEET MCQ: ‘angle of repose is equal to the angle of friction’ → TRUE
  • This equality holds only when static (limiting) friction is used, not kinetic
Example (NEET-style)If angle of friction = 37° (μs = tan 37° ≈ 0.75), then angle of repose = 37°. A slope steeper than 37° will cause the block to slide for this surface pair.

US Curriculum Gaps

AP Physics vs NEET on angle of repose.

‘Angle of Repose’ Not a Named Concept in AP Physics

AP Physics covers inclined plane friction (resolving mg cosθ and mg sinθ) but does not define the ‘angle of repose’ as a formal named quantity. NEET tests it as both a definition MCQ and a formula MCQ.

  • Memorise: angle of repose = angle at which body just begins to slide on inclined plane.
  • Formula: μs = tan (angle of repose). This must be derived by FBD on incline.

AP Physics Does Not Teach α = θ Equality

The equality between angle of repose and angle of friction (α = θ) is not in AP syllabi but is explicitly in NEET. This is a straightforward corollary: both angles have tan = μs; therefore they are equal.

  • In NEET MCQs: ‘which of the following is equal to the angle of friction?’ → angle of repose.
  • Both tan α and tan θ equal μs, so α = θ can be stated directly.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why is limiting (static) friction used in the derivation, not kinetic friction?
Angle of repose is the angle at which the body ‘just begins’ to slide — the onset of motion. At this instant, the friction force is at its maximum static value (limiting friction F_l = μs R). Once sliding starts, kinetic friction (μk < μs) takes over. The angle of repose corresponds to the verge of sliding, so limiting friction is correct.
Does the mass of the block affect the angle of repose?
No. In the derivation: mg sin α = μs mg cos α. The mass m cancels on both sides, giving tan α = μs, which is independent of m. The angle of repose depends only on the materials of the block and surface, not on the block’s mass or weight.
Is the angle of repose always equal to the angle of friction?
Yes, for a single body on a flat inclined surface (the standard NEET scenario): tan α = μs = tan θ, so α = θ. The NCERT explicitly states: ‘angle of repose = angle of friction’. This equality holds whenever the same coefficient of static friction governs both situations.
What happens if the slope angle exceeds the angle of repose?
If slope > angle of repose, mg sin α > μs mg cos α. Static friction cannot balance the gravitational component along the slope. The block starts sliding. Once sliding, kinetic friction μk < μs applies, and the net force along the slope is mg sin α − μk mg cos α > 0, so the block accelerates.
