Stopping of Block Due to Friction – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

A block moving with initial velocity u on a rough surface decelerates due to kinetic friction and comes to rest. On a horizontal surface: deceleration a = μg; stopping distance S = u²/(2μg); stopping time t = u/(μg). On a rough incline at angle θ: deceleration a = g(sinθ + μcosθ); stopping distance S = u²/[2g(sinθ + μcosθ)]. Key NEET insight: S is independent of mass (m cancels). The textbook also expresses S in terms of momentum: S = P²/(2μm²g), linking kinematics with momentum.

Difficulty⚡
Low-Medium
Concept is simple but NEET traps lie in the incline formula. On a horizontal surface, a = μg (pure friction). On an incline, a = g(sinθ + μcosθ) — gravity decelerates the block along the slope AND friction decelerates it. Students frequently forget the sinθ term and write only μcosθ. Also: mass cancels from S = u²/(2μg), so two blocks of different masses but same u and μ stop in the same distance.

Covered
AP Physics covers stopping distance on horizontal surfaces. However, NEET extends this to inclined planes with friction and requires the combined deceleration a = g(sinθ + μcosθ). The NEET-specific depth: expressing S in terms of initial momentum P = mu as S = P²/(2μm²g). US students will recognise the horizontal formula but may miss the momentum form and the incline generalisation.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Derive from Newton’s Second Law F_friction = μmg; ma = μmg; a = μg. Apply v² = u² − 2aS with v = 0: S = u²/(2μg). Apply v = u − at with v = 0: t = u/(μg). Both derivations take 30 seconds once the acceleration is known.

2

Extend to Inclined Surface On incline at angle θ, retardation is a = g(sinθ + μcosθ). Substituting into v² = u² − 2aS: S = u²/[2g(sinθ + μcosθ)]. Both gravity component (along incline) and friction act to decelerate the block moving upward. This is the NEET-critical extension.

3

Memorise Mass-Independence S = u²/(2μg) contains no mass term. A 5 kg block and a 50 kg block with the same u and same μ stop in identical distances. NEET sets questions where students incorrectly expect heavier blocks to stop farther (because larger momentum) — they do not, because friction is also larger in proportion.

1) Horizontal Surface Stopping

Must Memorise

Retarding force F = μmg; acceleration a = μg. From v² = u² − 2aS with v = 0: S = u²/(2μg). Also expressed as S = P²/(2μm²g) where P = mu. Stopping time t = u/(μg). Both S and t are independent of mass.

  • a = μg (retardation on horizontal rough surface)
  • S = u²/(2μg) — stopping distance, mass-independent
  • t = u/(μg) — stopping time, mass-independent
  • S = P²/(2μm²g) — momentum form
  • If u doubles, S quadruples (S ∝ u²)
Example (NEET-style)A car (u = 20 m/s, μ = 0.4): a = 0.4×10 = 4 m/s². S = 400/(2×4) = 50 m. t = 20/4 = 5 s.

2) Inclined Surface Stopping

NEET Extension

Block moves up a rough incline at angle θ with initial velocity u. Both gravity component (mg sinθ) and friction (mg μ cosθ) oppose motion. Total retardation a = g(sinθ + μcosθ). Stopping distance: S = u²/[2g(sinθ + μcosθ)].

  • Retardation on incline: a = g(sinθ + μcosθ)
  • S = u²/[2g(sinθ + μcosθ)]
  • Both sinθ and μcosθ decelerate the block
  • If θ = 0: reduces to S = u²/(2μg) — a good self-check
  • S given by the denominator sum, not difference
Example (NEET-style)θ = 30°, μ = 0.3, u = 10 m/s, g = 10 m/s². a = 10(0.5 + 0.3×√3/2) = 10(0.5 + 0.26) = 7.6 m/s². S = 100/(2×7.6) ≈ 6.6 m.

US Curriculum Gaps

How AP Physics compares to NEET on stopping-distance analysis.

Incline Stopping Not Standard in AP

AP Physics teaches stopping distance on horizontal surfaces but the incline formula with combined sinθ and μcosθ deceleration is not standard. NEET explicitly tests this combined form and expects immediate recall of S = u²/[2g(sinθ + μcosθ)] under examination conditions.

  • The ‘+’ sign in the denominator: both friction AND gravity oppose upward sliding.
  • For downward sliding on incline: deceleration = g(μcosθ − sinθ) if μ > tanθ.

Momentum Form of Stopping Distance

S = P²/(2μm²g) connects the stopping distance to initial momentum. This form appears in NEET problems that give P instead of u. US courses always work with velocity, not momentum, in stopping-distance problems.

  • Substitute P = mu into S = u²/(2μg) to get S = P²/(2μm²g).
  • If two blocks have equal momentum P but different masses, the lighter one stops in a greater distance (S ∝ 1/m² for fixed P).

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why doesn’t stopping distance depend on mass?
The friction force F_k = μmg is proportional to mass. The acceleration a = F_k/m = μg. The mass cancels, making a mass-independent. Since S = u²/(2a) = u²/(2μg), it also has no mass dependence. This contrasts with momentum: greater mass implies greater momentum at same u, but also proportionally greater friction to slow it down.
On an incline, why does the formula have sinθ + μcosθ in the denominator?
When a block moves up a rough incline, both gravity (component along incline = mg sinθ) and kinetic friction (f = μmg cosθ) oppose the motion. Total retarding force = mg(sinθ + μcosθ). Net deceleration a = g(sinθ + μcosθ), leading to S = u²/[2g(sinθ + μcosθ)].
What if the block slides down a rough incline? Is the formula different?
Yes. When sliding down, gravity accelerates the block (component mg sinθ) while friction decelerates it (f = μmg cosθ, opposing downward motion). Net acceleration = g(sinθ − μcosθ) if sinθ > μcosθ (i.e., angle > angle of repose). The stopping distance while moving upward should not be confused with the sliding-down case.
