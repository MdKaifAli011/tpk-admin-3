Friction as Cause of Motion – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

The common misconception that friction always opposes motion is a direct NEET trap. Friction can equally be the cause of forward motion: a walking person, a cycling rear wheel, and a body in an accelerating vehicle all move forward because of friction, not despite it. The critical condition for a body to stay at rest in an accelerating vehicle is ma < μmg, i.e., the acceleration must not exceed μg. NEET tests both the conceptual identification (which body has friction acting forward?) and the inequality condition.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Draw FBDs for All Three Scenarios Person walking: friction on foot is forward (ground pushes walker forward). Pedalling bicycle: rear wheel friction is forward; front wheel friction is backward. Accelerating vehicle: friction on loose body is forward (cause of its acceleration) provided ma < μmg.

2

Apply the Condition ma < μmg A body (mass m) rests on an accelerating vehicle (acceleration a). It will move with the vehicle, i.e., not slip, provided the required force ma is less than the maximum static friction μmg. If a > μg, the body slips backward relative to the vehicle.

3

Identify the Primary Conclusion The textbook conclusion: ‘without friction, motion cannot be started, stopped or transferred from one body to another.’ This is a direct NEET statement MCQ answer. Friction enables both the start (walking) and transfer (belt drive, bicycle) of motion.

Rapid Revision Cards

Concept → Trap → Example

1) Friction Enables Walking and Driving

Concept First

Walking: foot pushes ground backward (action); friction pushes foot forward (reaction). Vehicle rear wheel: wheel pushes ground backward; friction acts forward (propels vehicle). Front coasting wheel: friction acts backward (resists rolling). This is why a pedalled bicycle’s rear and front wheels have opposite friction directions.

  • Action-reaction pair: foot/wheel pushes ground; ground’s friction pushes back on foot/wheel
  • Rear wheel (driven): friction acts forward = cause of motion
  • Front wheel (coasting): friction acts backward = opposes rolling
  • If pedalling stopped: both wheels coast, both experience backward friction
  • Without friction, a vehicle’s wheels would spin in place (‘wheelspin’)
Example (NEET-style)In a car on wet ice (μs ≈ 0): driven wheels spin, car does not move. Add sand (increased μ) and traction is restored — friction is literally the cause of the vehicle’s acceleration.

2) Body on Accelerating Vehicle

Formula Card

A mass m on a vehicle accelerating at a: friction on m = ma (forward). Condition to not slip: ma ≤ μmg, i.e., a ≤ μg. If a > μg the body slides backward relative to vehicle.

  • Only friction provides the forward force to accelerate the body along with the vehicle
  • Maximum friction available = μmg (vertical N = mg on horizontal surface)
  • No-slip condition: ma ≤ μmg ⇒ a ≤ μg
  • If a > μg: body slides backward (friction becomes kinetic, still forward but insufficient)
  • NEET question type: find maximum vehicle acceleration for an object not to slip
Example (NEET-style)Block on truck floor, μs = 0.3, g = 10. Maximum safe acceleration = 0.3 × 10 = 3 m/s². If truck accelerates at 5 m/s², the block slips backward relative to the truck.

US Curriculum Gaps

AP Physics vs NEET on friction as cause of motion.

Bicycle Wheel Directionality

AP Physics covers traction in general but does not present the NEET-specific format of contrasting front vs rear wheel friction directions during pedalling vs coasting.

  • Pedalling: rear wheel friction = forward, front wheel friction = backward.
  • Coasting (no pedalling): both wheels have backward friction.
  • This is a favourite NEET FBD question — draw it once before the exam.

Explicit No-Slip Condition

AP Physics uses F = ma and frictional constraints, but NEET explicitly states the no-slip condition as ma < μmg and uses it as a direct MCQ formula. Memorise this inequality.

  • a_max = μg is the maximum vehicle acceleration for the block not to slide.
  • NEET MCQ: ‘A block rests on a truck with μ = 0.5. The maximum retardation for the block not to slide is —’ Answer: 5 m/s².

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
How can friction be the cause of motion if it always opposes relative motion?
Friction opposes relative motion BETWEEN the two surfaces in contact, not the absolute motion of the object. When your foot pushes the ground backward, the ground has a tendency to move backward relative to the foot. Friction opposes this tendency, so it acts forward on the foot. The foot’s absolute motion is forward — caused by friction. There is no contradiction.
What happens when a car accelerates on ice?
On ice, μ ≈ 0. The driven wheels can produce almost no traction force (friction ≈ 0), so the net horizontal force on the car body from the ground is near zero. The wheels spin but the car does not accelerate. This directly confirms that friction is the cause of vehicle acceleration — remove it and motion stops.
If a box on a truck does not slide, what is the friction force on the box?
If the truck accelerates at a m/s² and the box (mass m) does not slide, then the box also accelerates at a. The net force on the box = ma. The only horizontal force is static friction, so friction = ma (forward). This is less than μmg, so no sliding occurs.
In a bicycle, why do front and rear wheels experience friction in opposite directions?
The rear wheel is driven by the pedal mechanism: it pushes ground backward, so friction on it is forward (driving force). The front wheel is not powered: it rolls freely and has a tendency to decelerate, so friction acts backward on it, opposing its rolling. If pedalling stops, both wheels coast and both experience backward friction from the ground.
