Maximum Length of Hung Chain – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

A uniform chain of length l is placed on a rough table with length l’ hanging over the edge. Friction on the portion on the table must balance the weight of the hanging portion. The standard result uses the fact that for a uniform chain, mass is proportional to length, giving the coefficient of friction μ = l’ / (l − l’). Rearranging, the maximum fraction of chain that can hang without sliding is l’/l = μ/(1+μ). NEET tests this as a numerical or a conceptual question about what fraction can overhang given a specific μ.

Difficulty⚡
Low-Medium
The derivation is direct but students often forget to simplify to l’/l form. The typical NEET trap is finding l’ given l and μ and making arithmetic errors. Remember that l = l’ + l_table, so l’ = μ l / (1+μ). For μ = 1/3: l’ = l/4. For μ = 1/2: l’ = l/3.

Moderate Gap
US AP Physics does not test hanging-chain friction problems. This is a standard application of friction equilibrium to a continuous object (chain). The conceptual step that matters is: for a uniform chain, the fraction of mass hanging equals the fraction of length hanging. Once that is understood, the friction condition mg_hanging = μmg_table reduces to the length ratio formula.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Understand Why Length = Mass Fraction A uniform chain has constant linear mass density λ = m/l (kg/m). The mass of any segment of length x is λx. So mass ratio = length ratio. This is why μ = mass_hanging / mass_table directly becomes μ = l’ / (l − l’).

2

Derive Both Forms of the Result μ = l’/(l − l’) (direct form). Rearranged: l’ = μ(l − l’) = μl − μl’. So l’(1+μ) = μl, giving l’/l = μ/(1+μ). Commit both forms to memory.

3

Spot the NEET Numerical Shortcut Quick reference: μ = 1/4: l’ = l/5. μ = 1/3: l’ = l/4. μ = 1/2: l’ = l/3. μ = 1: l’ = l/2. For any μ, l’ = μl/(1+μ). Always check: should l’ < l? Yes, since μ/(1+μ) < 1 for all finite μ.

1) Hung Chain Equilibrium

Formula

For a uniform chain of length l, let l’ overhang the table and (l − l’) rest on it. At limiting equilibrium: weight of hanging part = maximum static friction. Since λ is constant: λg l’ = μ λg(l − l’). Cancelling λg: μ = l’/(l − l’). Rearranging: l’ = μl/(1+μ).

  • Valid only for uniform chain (constant linear density)
  • μ = l’/(l − l’) — hanging / on-table
  • l’/l = μ/(1+μ) — fraction overhung
  • If chain is non-uniform, mass fractions ≠ length fractions
Example (NEET-style)μ = 1/3, l = 90 cm. l’ = (1/3)×90 / (1 + 1/3) = 30/(4/3) = 22.5 cm.

US Curriculum Gaps

How AP Physics compares to NEET on hung-chain friction problems.

Continuous Object Friction Not in AP Scope

AP Physics does not systematically apply friction analysis to extended continuous objects like chains. The conceptual step that mass-fraction = length-fraction for a uniform chain is not tested. NEET expects students to both know this equivalence and apply it to find l’.

  • Memorise: for uniform chain, mass ∝ length, so μ = l’/(l − l’).
  • Derive l’ = μl/(1+μ) for exam-ready substitution.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why does the formula use length ratios directly instead of mass?
For a uniform chain, mass = λ × length where λ is the constant linear mass density. Both the hanging mass (λ l’) and the table mass (λ(l − l’)) have the same λ, which cancels when you write the friction equilibrium condition. So the mass ratio = length ratio, allowing the formula to be stated entirely in terms of lengths.
What happens if l’ > μl/(1+μ)?
If the hanging length exceeds the maximum l’ = μl/(1+μ), the friction force cannot support the weight of the hanging portion. The chain slides and continues to slide (kinetic friction now applies) until it falls off the table.
Can this approach be used for non-uniform chains?
Not directly. For a non-uniform chain, linear mass density λ(x) varies with position x. The mass of the hanging and on-table portions must be calculated by integrating λ(x) over the respective segments. The simple ratio μ = l’/(l − l’) does not apply.
