Stopping of Two Blocks – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

When two compressed blocks are suddenly released, they fly apart with equal and opposite momenta (P = −P by Newton’s third law). Each block’s kinetic energy is entirely dissipated by friction before it stops. Since both blocks have the same momentum magnitude P and the same coefficient of friction μ, the stopping distance formula S = P²/(2μm²g) shows that S ∝ 1/m². The ratio of their stopping distances is therefore S₁/S₂ = (m₂/m₁)². The heavier block stops sooner; the lighter block travels farther.

40 min

Difficulty⚡
Medium
Students often confuse equal momentum with equal kinetic energy. For a compressed system: momentum is equal in magnitude (P = mv), but kinetic energy KE = P²/2m differs — lighter block has MORE KE. The formula S ∝ 1/m² (not 1/m) surprises many students. Watch for m² vs m in the denominator.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Derive S = P²/(2μm²g) from First Principles Start with KE = ½mv² = μmgS. Substitute v = P/m: ½·m·(P/m)² = μmgS ⇒ P²/(2m) = μmgS ⇒ S = P²/(2μm²g). So S ∝ 1/m² for same P and μ.

2

Derive the Ratio S₁/S₂ = (m₂/m₁)² S₁/S₂ = [P²/(2μm₁²g)] / [P²/(2μm₂²g)] = m₂²/m₁² = (m₂/m₁)². The heavier block (m₂ > m₁) has a smaller stopping distance.

3

Apply to Numerical Problems m₁ = 1 kg, m₂ = 2 kg: S₁/S₂ = (2/1)² = 4. The lighter block stops 4x farther. Check: does result make physical sense? Lighter block has more KE (½m₁v₁² vs ½m₂v₂²: since P same, v₁ = 2v₂, KE₁ = 2×KE₂) but also less friction force (μm₁g < μm₂g). Both effects reinforce larger S₁.

1) Stopping Distance Ratio for Equal-Momentum Blocks

Must Memorise

Two compressed blocks released: momenta P₁ = −P₂ (magnitudes equal). Both experience friction coefficient μ on same surface. Energy dissipated by friction: μmgS = ½mv² = P²/(2m). So S = P²/(2μm²g). Both blocks have same |P| and same μ: S ∝ 1/m². Therefore S₁/S₂ = (m₂/m₁)².

  • Compressed system: |P₁| = |P₂| = P (momentum conservation from rest)
  • S = P² / (2μm²g)
  • S₁/S₂ = (m₂/m₁)²
  • S ∝ 1/m²: lighter block stops farther
  • S ∝ 1/μ: more friction ⇒ shorter stopping distance
Example (NEET-style)m₁ = 2 kg, m₂ = 4 kg, same μ, released from compressed spring. S₁/S₂ = (4/2)² = 4. Lighter (2 kg) block stops 4 times farther than heavier (4 kg) block.

US Curriculum Gaps

How AP Physics compares to NEET on two-block stopping distance.

Equal-Momentum Stopping Ratio Not Covered in AP Physics

AP Physics covers both friction and momentum independently but does not combine them in the compressed-spring release scenario to derive the S ∝ 1/m² result. The derivation using S = P²/(2μm²g) and the ratio (m₂/m₁)² is NEET-specific and requires deliberate practice for NRI students.

  • Key derivation step: substitute v = P/m into ½mv² = μmgS to get S = P²/(2μm²g).
  • Mass appears squared in denominator — a common mistake is writing 1/m instead of 1/m².

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why is the stopping distance proportional to 1/m² and not 1/m?
Because momentum P = mv is fixed, the velocity is v = P/m. KE = ½mv² = ½m(P/m)² = P²/(2m). The friction force is f = μmg. Stopping distance S = KE/f = [P²/(2m)] / (μmg) = P²/(2μm²g). Mass appears in both the KE (as 1/m) and the friction force (as m), giving m² in the denominator overall. Hence S ∝ 1/m².
What if the two blocks have different coefficients of friction?
If μ₁ ≠ μ₂, then S₁/S₂ = (μ₂m₂²)/(μ₁m₁²). Both μ and m² appear in the ratio. The special case S₁/S₂ = (m₂/m₁)² applies ONLY when both blocks are on the same surface (μ₁ = μ₂).
The lighter block has more kinetic energy, yet S ∝ 1/m² — is that consistent?
Yes. KE = P²/(2m): lighter block has more KE (more energy to dissipate). But friction force = μmg is also smaller for a lighter block (less friction). The net effect: lighter block’s extra energy outweighs its reduced friction, so it stops farther. The factor is S₁/S₂ = (m₂/m₁)², not (m₂/m₁) as intuition might suggest.
