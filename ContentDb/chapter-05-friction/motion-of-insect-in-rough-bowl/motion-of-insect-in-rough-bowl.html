Motion of Insect in Rough Bowl – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

An insect crawls up the rough inner surface of a hemispherical bowl of radius r. It can climb only up to a height h where the limiting friction is just enough to oppose the component of the insect’s weight along the surface. At the highest point reached, R = mg cosθ and Fl = mg sinθ, giving tanθ = μ. Using geometry: h = r[1 − 1/√(1+μ²)]. NEET tests this formula directly and as a geometry-plus-friction derivation MCQ.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Derive Height Formula from Scratch Step 1: At angle θ from vertical, height h = r − r cosθ = r(1 − cosθ). Step 2: Equilibrium along surface gives Fl = mg sinθ and R = mg cosθ. Step 3: Limiting condition: Fl = μR ⇒ tanθ = μ. Step 4: From tanθ = μ draw right triangle: cosθ = 1/√(1+μ²). Step 5: h = r(1 − 1/√(1+μ²)).

2

Memorise the Formula h = r[1 − 1/√(1+μ²)]. As μ → 0: h → 0 (insect can’t climb smooth bowl). As μ → ∞: h → r (insect climbs to top). This behaviour check confirms formula correctness in NEET MCQs.

3

Apply Extreme Cases as Verification Check: when μ = 1, h = r(1 − 1/√2) = r(1 − 0.707) = 0.293r. At μ = 0: h = 0. At very large μ: h approaches r. These sanity checks confirm formula application without computing from scratch.

1) Bowl Geometry and Limiting Condition

Must Memorise

Insect of mass m in bowl of radius r climbs to angle θ from vertical. At this point: normal reaction R = mg cosθ (perpendicular to bowl surface), friction Fl = mg sinθ (along surface, upward). Limiting condition: tanθ = μ. From this, cosθ = 1/√(1+μ²). Height: h = r − r cosθ = r[1 − 1/√(1+μ²)].

  • h = r[1 − 1/√(1+μ²)] — maximum height insect can climb
  • Limiting condition: tanθ = μ (angle of friction)
  • cosθ = 1/√(1+μ²) at limiting condition (from right triangle with tanθ = μ)
  • Height formula: h = r(1 − cosθ) from bowl geometry
  • At μ = 0: h = 0; at μ → ∞: h → r (sanity checks)
Example (NEET-style)Bowl radius r = 0.5 m, μ = 0.75. cosθ = 1/√(1+0.5625) = 1/√1.5625 = 1/1.25 = 0.8. h = 0.5(1 − 0.8) = 0.5×0.2 = 0.1 m. The insect climbs 10 cm above the lowest point of the bowl.

2) Physical Interpretation and Limiting Cases

Conceptual

At the highest point, gravity component along the surface (mg sinθ) exactly equals limiting friction (Fl = μmg cosθ). Above this angle, friction cannot balance gravity’s along-surface component and the insect slides down. The insect’s maximum height is therefore set by the equality tanθ = μ, which is the condition that defines the angle of friction itself.

  • Insect slides when mg sinθ > μmg cosθ, i.e., tanθ > μ
  • Maximum height is where tanθ = μ (limiting friction exactly balances gravity component)
  • Greater μ allows higher climbing — h increases with μ (non-linearly)
  • The angle of maximum climb = angle of friction = tan⁻¹(μ)
  • C onnection to angle of repose: same balance condition applies
Example (NEET-style)If μ = 1 (very rough): cosθ = 1/√2, so h = r(1 − 1/√2) ≈ 0.293r. If μ = 2: cosθ = 1/√5 ≈ 0.447, h ≈ 0.553r. Higher μ enables more climb but never reaches the top (h < r always for finite μ).

US Curriculum Gaps

How AP Physics and US curricula compare to NEET scope on bowl-insect friction problems.

Bowl-Insect Problem Not in AP Physics

AP Physics does not cover the bowl-climbing insect scenario. Students need to practise the combination of circular bowl geometry (h = r − r cosθ) with the friction equilibrium condition (R = mg cosθ, Fl = mg sinθ, tanθ = μ) as an integrated two-step derivation unique to NEET scope.

  • Step 1: Use h = r(1 − cosθ) from circular geometry.
  • Step 2: Apply tanθ = μ ⇒ cosθ = 1/√(1+μ²) to get final h.

Connection to Angle of Friction

The limiting condition tanθ = μ in this problem is precisely the definition of the angle of friction. Recognising this link makes the derivation trivial: the insect climbs until the bowl’s surface angle equals the angle of friction. US curricula do not make this link explicit in problem-solving contexts.

  • Maximum climb angle = angle of friction = tan⁻¹(μ).
  • This is the same condition as angle of repose for inclined plane.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why is the limiting condition tanθ = μ and not some other expression?
At angle θ from vertical in the bowl, the two force components along the surface are mg sinθ (gravity, directed down-surface) and the friction force Fl (directed up-surface to support the insect). Normal to the surface: R = mg cosθ. At the limit of climbing, friction equals its maximum: Fl = μR = μmg cosθ. Balancing along surface: μmg cosθ = mg sinθ ⇒ tanθ = μ. This is the angle of friction.
How is the height formula derived from tanθ = μ?
From tanθ = μ, draw a right triangle with opposite = μ, adjacent = 1, hypotenuse = √(1+μ²). Therefore cosθ = 1/√(1+μ²). Height above bowl bottom: h = r − r cosθ = r(1 − cosθ) = r[1 − 1/√(1+μ²)]. The second step uses circular geometry: the projection of radius r along vertical = r cosθ, so height above bottom = r − r cosθ.
Can the insect ever reach the rim of the bowl?
Only if μ → ∞. For any finite μ, cosθ = 1/√(1+μ²) > 0, so h = r(1 − cosθ) < r. The insect always falls short of the rim by r cosθ = r/√(1+μ²). For a very rough surface (μ = 10): h ≈ r(1 − 1/√101) ≈ r(1 − 0.0995) ≈ 0.9r. Still below the rim.
Why does the normal reaction equal mg cosθ and not the total weight mg?
The bowl surface is curved; at angle θ from vertical, the surface normal points radially inward (toward the bowl centre). Weight mg acts vertically downward. The component of mg perpendicular to the bowl surface = mg cosθ (the projection of mg along the inward radius). The component along the surface = mg sinθ. Hence R = mg cosθ, not mg.
