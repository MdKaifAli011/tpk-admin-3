Acceleration of Block Against Friction – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

When a block slides on a rough inclined plane, kinetic friction acts opposite to motion and modifies the net acceleration. Sliding down a rough incline of angle θ (θ > angle of repose): a = g(sinθ − μ cosθ). Sliding up (or decelerating) on the same incline: a = g(sinθ + μ cosθ). For horizontal surfaces, a = (P − Fk)/m under an applied force. NEET tests both inclined-plane accelerations and the critical condition for motion to begin (θ > angle of repose).

Difficulty⚡
Medium
Straightforward FBD algebra, but direction of friction is the key trap. NEET trap: when a block is projected UP an incline, gravity AND kinetic friction both oppose motion (both act downward along plane), giving retardation a = g(sinθ + μ cosθ). Students commonly subtract friction instead of adding it. Always ask: in which direction is the block actually moving, then assign friction opposite to that direction.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Derive Both Cases from FBD Case 1 (sliding down): Net force along plane = mg sinθ − μmg cosθ. a = g(sinθ − μ cosθ). Case 2 (retardation upward): Both gravity component and friction act downward along plane. Retardation = g(sinθ + μ cosθ).

2

Check the Motion-Start Condition Motion down the incline starts only if θ > angle of repose (i.e., tanθ > μ). If tanθ ≤ μ, the block stays stationary and a = 0. Always check this before computing down-slope acceleration.

3

Apply to Horizontal Surface On a horizontal surface with applied force P and kinetic friction Fk = μmg: ma = P − μmg, so a = (P/m) − μg. If P is removed, retardation = μg (block decelerates due to friction alone).

Rapid Revision Cards

Concept → Trap → Example

1) Acceleration Down Rough Incline

Must Memorise

Block sliding down rough incline of angle θ where tanθ > μ (i.e. θ > angle of repose): FBD gives ma = mg sinθ − F_k = mg sinθ − μmg cosθ. Therefore a = g(sinθ − μ cosθ). For frictionless incline (μ = 0): a = g sinθ. Friction REDUCES down-slope acceleration.

  • a = g(sinθ − μ cosθ) when sliding down rough incline
  • Condition for motion: tanθ > μ (otherwise block stays still)
  • At θ = angle of repose (tanθ = μ): a = 0 (barely moves)
  • Friction acts UP the incline opposing downward sliding
  • Normal reaction R = mg cosθ; kinetic friction Fk = μmg cosθ
Example (NEET-style)θ = 30°, μ = 0.2, g = 10 m/s². Check: tan30° = 0.577 > 0.2 so motion occurs. a = 10(sin30° − 0.2×cos30°) = 10(0.5 − 0.2×0.866) = 10(0.5 − 0.173) = 10×0.327 = 3.27 m/s².

2) Retardation Sliding Up Rough Incline

Exam Trap

Block projected up a rough incline: both gravity component (mg sinθ downward along plane) and friction (Fk = μmg cosθ downward along plane, opposing upward motion) act in the same direction. Net retardation = g(sinθ + μ cosθ). This is always greater than retardation on smooth incline (g sinθ), so the block decelerates faster going up than it accelerates going down.

  • Retardation = g(sinθ + μ cosθ) when projected up rough incline
  • Friction now acts DOWN the incline (opposing upward motion)
  • Both gravity & friction components oppose upward motion — both subtract from up-velocity
  • Up-incline retardation > down-incline acceleration (always, for same rough incline)
  • Time to go up < time to come down on rough incline
Example (NEET-style)θ = 30°, μ = 0.2, g = 10 m/s². Retardation going up = 10(sin30° + 0.2×cos30°) = 10(0.5 + 0.173) = 6.73 m/s². Compare: acceleration going down = 3.27 m/s². Block decelerates twice as fast going up as it accelerates going down.

US Curriculum Gaps

How AP Physics and US curricula compare to NEET on inclined-plane friction acceleration.

Sign Convention for Inclined Friction Not Explicit in AP

AP Physics expects students to work from FBD each time. NEET expects instant recall of a = g(sinθ ± μ cosθ) with the sign determined by motion direction. The ‘+’ vs ‘−’ in the formula captures opposing friction directions, a distinction AP Physics leaves implicit.

  • Memorise: minus sign for sliding down (θ > repose angle), plus sign for going up.
  • Verify: friction direction (always oppose motion) before substituting the formula.

Comparison Between Up and Down Incline Motions

NEET frequently asks to compare time to slide down from rest vs. time to slide back up from bottom, requiring both acceleration formulas simultaneously. AP Physics does not typically frame problems this way.

  • Down: a₂ = g(sinθ − μ cosθ); Up: a₁ = g(sinθ + μ cosθ). Note a₁ > a₂.
  • For same distance s: t_up < t_down because retardation (a₁) > acceleration (a₂).

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
What happens if the incline angle equals the angle of repose?
At θ = angle of repose, tanθ = μ. The acceleration formula gives a = g(sinθ − μ cosθ) = g cosθ(tanθ − μ) = 0. The block is on the verge of motion — it will just barely begin to slide and any slight tap will cause sliding, but it will not accelerate on its own.
Why is retardation going up greater than acceleration going down for the same incline?
Going down, gravity helps (⬇) and friction opposes (⬆): net = mg sinθ − μmg cosθ. Going up, gravity opposes (⬇) and friction also opposes (⬇): net retarding force = mg sinθ + μmg cosθ. The friction term is added in both cases, but in opposite directions relative to motion, so going up sees a larger net retarding force.
Can a block on a rough incline have zero acceleration while moving?
No — if θ > angle of repose and the block is sliding, kinetic friction is present. The acceleration = g(sinθ − μ cosθ) > 0, so the block speeds up as it slides down. Zero acceleration while moving would require exactly g(sinθ) = μg cosθ at every instant, which means tanθ = μ — the borderline case.
How does the horizontal-surface acceleration relate to the inclined-plane case?
On a horizontal surface (θ = 0), a = g(sin0 − μ cos0) = −μg. This means a decelerating block (no applied force) has retardation μg. With an applied force P: ma = P − μmg, so a = P/m − μg. The incline formulas reduce correctly to the horizontal case at θ = 0.
