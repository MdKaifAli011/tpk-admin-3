Sticking of Block With Accelerated Cart – Complete Notes, Revision, Important Questions & Downloads

A block rests on the vertical face of an accelerated cart. When the cart accelerates to the right with acceleration a, a pseudo-force ma acts on the block toward the left (in the cart’s reference frame), pressing the block against the vertical face. This pressing force provides the normal reaction (R = ma) that generates friction (f = μma). For the block not to fall, friction must support its weight: μma ≥ mg, giving the critical condition a₂min = g/μ. This is a classic NEET application of the pseudo-force concept combined with vertical friction equilibrium.

Difficulty⚡
Medium
The main challenge is recognising that friction acts vertically and the normal force acts horizontally, which reverses the typical orientation. In standard friction problems, normal force is vertical and friction is horizontal. Here it is the opposite. Students who draw the wrong FBD get the condition backwards. Also note: the block’s weight and the cart’s total mass (M+m) determine the minimum force on the cart: F_min = (M+m)g/μ.

NEET Mastery Strategy

1

Draw the FBD in the Accelerating Frame Block on vertical face of cart: pseudo-force ma acts horizontally (left) on block, pressing it against the cart’s vertical surface. Normal reaction R = ma (horizontal, rightward). Friction f = μR = μma acts vertically upward. Weight mg acts vertically downward. Equilibrium: f ≥ mg ⇒ μma ≥ mg ⇒ a ≥ g/μ.

2

Derive F_min for the Cart The minimum force F applied to the cart of mass M with block of mass m: F = (M+m)a_min = (M+m)g/μ. This uses Newton’s second law for the cart+block system. F_min = (M+m)g/μ is the NEET-extension formula.

3

Check Mass-Independence of a_min a_min = g/μ has no dependence on the mass of the block m. Heavier or lighter block—the minimum cart acceleration is always g/μ. This is the analogue of the stopping-distance mass-independence result and a common NEET conceptual trap.

1) Cart-Block Sticking Condition

Must Memorise

A block m is on the vertical face of a cart M accelerating rightward at a. In the cart frame, pseudo-force ma acts leftward on block. Normal reaction R = ma (provided by cart’s vertical surface). Kinetic/static friction f = μR = μma acts upward. For block not to fall: μma ≥ mg. Therefore a_min = g/μ. Minimum cart force: F_min = (M+m)g/μ.

  • Pseudo-force concept: ma horizontal, pressing block to vertical face
  • Normal reaction R = ma (horizontal)
  • Friction f = μma (vertical, upward)
  • a_min = g/μ — independent of block mass
  • F_min = (M+m)g/μ — depends on total mass
Example (NEET-style)μ = 0.5, m = 2 kg, M = 8 kg, g = 10 m/s². a_min = 10/0.5 = 20 m/s². F_min = 10×20 = 200 N.

US Curriculum Gaps

How AP Physics compares to NEET on the accelerated-cart block-sticking problem.

Reversed FBD Orientation Not Standard in AP

In this problem, normal force is horizontal and friction is vertical — opposite to the standard orientation. AP Physics students trained only on horizontal surface friction problems will draw the FBD incorrectly. This conceptual reversal must be explicitly practised for NEET.

  • In cart frame: pseudo-force → horizontal normal → vertical friction.
  • Always label which direction friction acts (upward here, opposing gravity).

F_min Formula for Total System

AP Physics may compute normal forces and friction for systems, but the specific result F_min = (M+m)g/μ for the minimum applied force on a cart to keep a block from sliding is a NEET-specific application.

  • F = (M+m)a_min where a_min = g/μ.
  • The formula provides both a_min and F_min in one step.

Frequently Asked Questions

Notes · Downloads · Revision · Important Questions
Why does a_min not depend on the mass of the block?
The vertical equilibrium is μma = mg. Dividing both sides by m gives μa = g, so a = g/μ. The mass m cancels. This is analogous to stopping distance’s mass-independence: friction and inertia are both proportional to mass, so mass drops out of the equilibrium condition.
What happens if a > a_min?
If the cart accelerates faster than a_min = g/μ, the friction force μma exceeds the required mg. The block presses harder against the vertical face, generating even more friction. The block remains firmly attached and moves with the cart. There is no issue with excess acceleration — only insufficient acceleration causes the block to fall.
Can this problem be solved in the ground (inertial) frame?
Yes. In the inertial frame, the horizontal forces on the block are: normal force R from the cart wall (horizontal, say rightward) and the horizontal component of friction (none, since friction is vertical). The net horizontal force on the block = R = ma (Newton’s second law). Vertical: f − mg = 0 ⇒ μR − mg = 0 ⇒ μma = mg ⇒ a = g/μ. Same result.
