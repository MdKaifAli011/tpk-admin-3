Chapter Snapshot - Kinetic Theory of Gases

A deeply quantitative chapter where microscopic molecular motion is linked to macroscopic gas properties. Ideal gas equation PV = nRT = NkT, the pressure formula P = ⅓ρv²_rms, and the three molecular speeds — v_rms, v_mean, v_mp in increasing order — are the most-tested formulas, appearing in almost every NEET paper. Degrees of freedom and the equipartition theorem (KE per degree = ½kT) directly determine Cv, Cp, and γ for monatomic, diatomic, and polyatomic gases — a trio of values that NEET tests both directly and inside thermodynamics questions. Mastering the speed-order inequality v_mp < v_mean < v_rms and the rigid-diatomic f = 5 rule eliminates the two most expensive errors in this chapter.

Subtopics - Kinetic Theory of Gases (NEET)

Four major blocks: the ideal gas model and its molecular interpretation (PV = nRT, Boltzmann constant, Avogadro's number), the kinetic theory derivation of pressure and the complete set of molecular speed expressions (v_rms, v_mean, v_mp), degrees of freedom and the equipartition theorem leading to specific heats and γ for all molecule types, and the real gas correction via the van der Waals equation.

Revision tip: Before any kinetic theory numerical, identify which speed formula is needed (rms uses √3, mean uses √8/π, most probable uses √2) and whether the answer is per molecule (use k) or per mole (use R). These two checks prevent the most common calculation errors. For degrees-of-freedom questions ask: is the molecule monatomic (f=3), rigid diatomic (f=5), or polyatomic (f=6)? — NEET almost always means rigid diatomic.

1) Ideal Gas Law and Molecular Speeds

Derives the macroscopic ideal gas law PV = nRT from microscopic assumptions and extends it to PV = NkT (k = R/NA = 1.38×10⁻²³ J/K). Establishes the complete set of molecular speed distributions: root mean square speed v_rms = √(3RT/M) = √(3kT/m), mean speed v_mean = √(8RT/πM), most probable speed v_mp = √(2RT/M). The speed ordering is v_mp < v_mean < v_rms always. All three speeds scale as √T and as 1/√M — so lighter gases and higher temperatures both shift all speeds upward proportionally.

PV = nRT = NkTv_rms = √(3RT/M)v_mp < v_mean < v_rmsv ∝ √T, v ∝ 1/√M

Ideal Gas Equation and Boltzmann ConstantIdeal gas law: PV = nRT (n = moles, R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹) and equivalently PV = NkT (N = number of molecules, k = R/NA = 1.38×10⁻²³ J/K). At STP (T = 273 K, P = 1 atm) one mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 L and contains NA = 6.023×10²³ molecules. Gas density ρ = PM/(RT) — density is proportional to molar mass and pressure, inversely proportional to temperature. Useful form: P = ρkT/m (m = mass of one molecule). Mixture: total pressure = sum of partial pressures (Dalton's law). Temperature in ALL gas law calculations must be in Kelvin.
›
Three Molecular Speeds and Their RatiosRoot mean square speed: v_rms = √(3RT/M) = √(3kT/m) = √(3P/ρ). Mean (average) speed: v_mean = √(8RT/πM) = √(8kT/πm). Most probable speed: v_mp = √(2RT/M) = √(2kT/m). Ratio: v_rms : v_mean : v_mp = √3 : √(8/π) : √2 ≈ 1.732 : 1.596 : 1.414. Order: v_mp is smallest, v_rms is largest — always. All three ∝ √T (doubling temperature multiplies speeds by √2, not 2). All three ∝ 1/√M (oxygen with M=32 has speeds 4 times smaller than hydrogen with M=2 at same T). For oxygen at NTP: v_rms = 461 m/s, v_mean = 424.7 m/s, v_mp = 376.4 m/s. v_rms is used in pressure and energy derivations; v_mp is the peak of the Maxwell distribution curve.

2) Kinetic Theory Pressure Derivation

Derives gas pressure P from first principles by computing the momentum transferred to container walls by N molecules in a cubic box of side L. Key steps: each molecule with x-velocity v_x transfers momentum 2mv_x per collision; collision frequency = v_x/2L; single-molecule force = mv²_x/L; sum over all N molecules and divide by area gives P = (1/3)(mN/V)v²_rms = (1/3)ρv²_rms. From this derives KE per unit volume E = (3/2)P, and kinetic energy per molecule = (3/2)kT = (1/2)mv²_rms, and per mole = (3/2)RT. The factor ⅓ arises from averaging over all three coordinate directions.

P = ⅓ρv²_rmsKE/molecule = ½mv²_rms = 3/2 kTKE/mole = 3/2 RTP = 2/3 E (energy density)
›
Pressure Derivation and Energy DensityPressure formula: P = (1/3)(mN/V)v²_rms = (1/3)ρv²_rms. Kinetic energy per unit volume: E = (1/2)ρv²_rms, therefore P = (2/3)E — pressure equals two-thirds of translational KE density. KE per molecule: K = (1/2)mv²_rms = (3/2)kT. KE per mole: K_molar = (3/2)RT. Since KE ∝ T (Kelvin), doubling temperature from 300 K to 600 K doubles KE but multiplies v_rms by only √2 ≈ 1.41, not 2. Effects on pressure: P ∝ T at constant V; P ∝ 1/V at constant T; P ∝ N/V (number density). Relation between pressure and speed of sound: v_sound = √(γP/ρ); ratio v_sound/v_rms = √(γ/3).
›
Mean Free Path and Molecular Collision FrequencyMean free path: λ = 1/(√2 π d² n) where d = molecular diameter, n = number density (N/V). λ is proportional to 1/(n·d²): decreases as gas density or molecule size increases. Collision frequency: z = √2 π d² n v_mean. At NTP average intermolecular distance ≈ 10⁻⁹ m. Mean free path is much larger than molecular diameter. λ increases when pressure decreases (density decreases) or temperature increases at constant pressure. Applications: viscosity, thermal conductivity, and diffusion all depend on λ.

3) Degrees of Freedom and Law of Equipartition

Defines degrees of freedom f as the number of independent coordinates needed to specify a molecule's configuration. Monatomic (He, Ne, Ar): f = 3 (translational only). Rigid diatomic (H₂, O₂, N₂ at NEET temperatures): f = 5 (3 translational + 2 rotational). Non-rigid diatomic (high temperature): f = 7 (adds 2 vibrational). Triatomic nonlinear (H₂O): f = 6. Equipartition theorem: each degree of freedom contributes ½kT of KE per molecule. From this Cv = (f/2)R; Cp = (f/2 + 1)R; γ = Cp/Cv = 1 + 2/f. NEET always uses rigid diatomic f = 5 giving γ = 7/5 = 1.4. Monatomic γ = 5/3; diatomic γ = 7/5; triatomic linear γ = 4/3.

Monatomic f=3, γ=5/3Diatomic rigid f=5, γ=7/5γ = 1 + 2/fCv = (f/2)R, Cp = (f/2+1)R
›
Degrees of Freedom for All Molecule TypesMonatomic gas (He, Ne, Ar): f = 3 (three translational). KE per molecule = (3/2)kT; KE per mole = (3/2)RT. Rigid diatomic gas (H₂, O₂, N₂, CO): f = 5 (3 translational + 2 rotational about axes perpendicular to bond axis; rotation about bond axis is neglected for rigid model). Non-rigid diatomic at high temperature: f = 7 (adds 2 vibrational degrees). Linear triatomic (CO₂): f = 5. Nonlinear triatomic (H₂O, SO₂): f = 6. Solid: each atom has 3 vibrational modes each with KE and PE degrees = 6f total; molar heat capacity = 3R (Dulong-Petit law). NEET context: rigid diatomic f = 5 is used in all standard problems unless explicitly stated otherwise.
›
Equipartition Theorem Specific Heats and γEquipartition theorem: each quadratic degree of freedom stores (1/2)kT average energy per molecule = (1/2)RT per mole. Molar specific heat at constant volume: Cv = (f/2)R. Molar specific heat at constant pressure: Cp = Cv + R = (f/2 + 1)R (Mayer's relation). Ratio γ = Cp/Cv = 1 + 2/f. Summary table — Monatomic: f=3, Cv=(3/2)R, Cp=(5/2)R, γ=5/3≈1.67. Rigid diatomic: f=5, Cv=(5/2)R, Cp=(7/2)R, γ=7/5=1.4. Triatomic nonlinear: f=6, Cv=3R, Cp=4R, γ=4/3≈1.33. As f increases both Cv and Cp increase but γ decreases. For water (treated as solid): C ≈ 9R per mole. Cv = R/(γ-1); Cp = γR/(γ-1). These follow directly from γ definition.

4) Real Gases and van der Waals Equation

Introduces corrections to the ideal gas model for real behaviour: intermolecular attraction reduces effective pressure by a/V² (adds a/V² to measured P); finite molecular volume reduces available volume by nb (uses V-nb). Combined van der Waals equation: (P + a/V²)(V - b) = RT per mole. Constant a accounts for intermolecular attraction (larger a = stronger attraction); constant b accounts for finite molecular size (b = 4NA × volume of one molecule). At low pressure and high temperature real gases approach ideal behaviour. Unsaturated vapours obey gas laws; saturated vapours do not. Free expansion of a real gas causes temperature to decrease (Joule-Thomson effect) because molecules must overcome attractive forces.

(P+a/V²)(V-b)=RTa → attraction correctionb → volume correctionFree expansion: T decreases for real gas
›
Van der Waals Equation and Its ConstantsVan der Waals equation per mole: (P + a/V²)(V - b) = RT. Per n moles: (P + n²a/V²)(V - nb) = nRT. Correction term a/V²: effective pressure is less than ideal because intermolecular attractions pull molecules away from the wall before impact; adding a/V² to measured P recovers ideal pressure. Correction term b: effective volume is reduced by the excluded volume of molecules; b = 4NA × (4/3)πr³ where r is molecular radius. At very high pressure or low volume the b-correction dominates (gas is hard to compress). At moderate conditions the a-correction dominates (gas easier to compress than expected due to attractions at intermediate densities). Critical temperature Tc: above Tc the gas cannot be liquefied by pressure alone. Real gas behaviour: when van der Waals gas expands freely into vacuum, temperature drops because KE converts to PE against intermolecular attractions; ideal gas temperature unchanged in free expansion.
›
Comparison of Ideal and Real Gas BehaviourIdeal gas assumptions violated in real gases: (1) molecules have finite volume → b correction; (2) intermolecular forces are non-zero → a correction. PV/RT = Z (compressibility factor): Z=1 for ideal gas; Z<1 at moderate pressure (attraction dominates); Z>1 at very high pressure (repulsion and finite volume dominate). Boyle temperature TB = a/(Rb): at this temperature Z≈1 over a wide pressure range. At low pressure and high temperature all real gases behave ideally. Saturated vapour obeys neither Boyle's law nor Charles's law because condensation occurs. Unsaturated vapour (below saturation) approximately follows gas laws.

Molecular Speed Order — v_rms > v_mean > v_mp (Not the Reverse)

Mistake Snapshot (What Students Do Wrong)

  • Writing v_rms < v_mean < v_mp (completely reversed order):: The correct order is v_mp < v_mean < v_rms. v_rms is the LARGEST of the three speeds. Students who confuse 'most probable' with 'largest' flip the order entirely. Numerically: v_rms ≈ 1.73√(RT/M), v_mean ≈ 1.60√(RT/M), v_mp ≈ 1.41√(RT/M) — the coefficients directly confirm which is largest.
  • Confusing v_mp with v_rms in the ideal gas pressure formula:: Pressure P = (1/3)ρv²_rms uses v_rms only. Substituting v_mp = √(2RT/M) instead of v_rms = √(3RT/M) gives a pressure reduced by a factor 2/3. The pressure derivation requires the mean-square speed, which is v²_rms by definition — not v²_mp.
2–3 Line Example (Typical Error)

At 300 K, for O₂ (M = 32×10⁻³ kg/mol): v_rms = √(3×8.314×300/0.032) = 484 m/s; v_mean = √(8×8.314×300/π×0.032) = 446 m/s; v_mp = √(2×8.314×300/0.032) = 395 m/s. Order confirmed: 395 < 446 < 484. NEET asks which is largest and provides 'v_mp' as the most attractive wrong answer.

How NEET Frames The Trap

NEET typically phrases this as 'Arrange the following speeds in increasing order' or 'Which of the following represents the correct relationship?' NEET provides all four orderings as options, making it a guaranteed mark for students who recall the coefficient rule.

NEET-Style Trap Question Format

Q. For a gas at temperature T, which of the following correctly shows the relationship between root mean square speed (v_rms), mean speed (v_avg), and most probable speed (v_mp)?
A. v_mp < v_avg < v_rms   B. v_rms < v_avg < v_mp   C. v_avg < v_mp < v_rms   D. v_mp < v_rms < v_avg  
Trick: The coefficients under √(RT/M) are: v_rms uses √3 ≈ 1.73, v_avg uses √(8/π) ≈ 1.60, v_mp uses √2 ≈ 1.41. Largest coefficient = largest speed. So v_mp is smallest, v_rms is largest. Answer: Option A.

Quick rule: Coefficient rule: 3 for rms, 8/π for mean, 2 for most-probable. Since 3 > 8/π > 2, the order is v_rms > v_mean > v_mp always. Equivalently: v_mp is smallest, v_rms is largest. One number — 3 > 8/π > 2 — gives the complete ordering.

Mistake Snapshot (What Students Do Wrong)

  • Claiming that doubling temperature doubles v_rms:: v_rms ∝ √T, not T. Doubling T from 300 K to 600 K multiplies v_rms by √(600/300) = √2 ≈ 1.41, not 2. Students who write v_rms ∝ T will get a factor of 2 instead of √2 — a very common NEET error. KE = (1/2)mv²_rms ∝ T DO double when T doubles because KE depends on v², not v.
  • Using temperature in Celsius instead of Kelvin in speed formulas:: All kinetic theory formulas require absolute temperature in Kelvin. A problem at 27°C means T = 300 K, not 27. v_rms = √(3RT/M) with T in Celsius gives a completely wrong result. NEET consistently provides temperature in °C in problem stems to force a Kelvin conversion.
2–3 Line Example (Typical Error)

v_rms at 300 K = 400 m/s. Find v_rms at 1200 K. Using v ∝ √T: v_new = 400 × √(1200/300) = 400 × √4 = 400 × 2 = 800 m/s. WRONG approach — claiming T quadrupled so v doubled is correct by coincidence here only because √4 = 2. For non-perfect-square ratios like 300 K to 600 K: v_new = 400 × √2 ≈ 566 m/s, not 800 m/s.

How NEET Frames The Trap

NEET gives initial v_rms at one temperature and asks for v_rms when temperature is doubled or quadrupled. Options include the ×2 answer (wrong for doubled T) and the ×√2 answer (correct for doubled T). The factor-of-2 option is placed first and looks more natural.

NEET-Style Trap Question Format

Q. The rms speed of molecules of a gas at 27°C is 200 m/s. What will be the rms speed at 927°C?
A. 400 m/s   B. 200√2 m/s   C. 800 m/s   D. 100√2 m/s  
Trick: T₁ = 27 + 273 = 300 K; T₂ = 927 + 273 = 1200 K. v_rms ∝ √T. v₂/v₁ = √(1200/300) = √4 = 2. v₂ = 2 × 200 = 400 m/s. Option A is correct. Option C (800 m/s) is the trap answer for students who think v ∝ T. Also note: temperatures must be in Kelvin.

Quick rule: Two-step check: (1) convert °C to Kelvin; (2) use v_rms ∝ √T, KE ∝ T. Speed scales as square root of temperature; energy scales linearly. To get new speed: v_new = v_old × √(T_new / T_old).
Rigid Diatomic Has f = 5, Not f = 7 — NEET Always Uses Rigid
NEET 2017NEET 2020Degrees of freedomDiatomic gasγ value trap

Mistake Snapshot (What Students Do Wrong)

  • Using f = 7 for diatomic gas in NEET problems giving γ = 9/7:: f = 7 applies to a non-rigid diatomic at very high temperature (3 translational + 2 rotational + 2 vibrational). NEET always uses the rigid diatomic model (f = 5) giving γ = 7/5 = 1.4 and Cv = (5/2)R. Using f = 7 gives the wrong γ = 1 + 2/7 = 9/7 ≈ 1.29 and wrong specific heats. No NEET question uses f = 7 unless explicitly mentioning high-temperature vibrations.
  • Using γ = 5/3 (monatomic value) for a diatomic gas:: γ = 5/3 applies ONLY to monatomic gases (He, Ne, Ar, Hg vapour). For H₂, O₂, N₂, CO — all diatomic — γ = 7/5. This error propagates catastrophically into adiabatic process calculations where PV^γ = constant: using γ = 5/3 instead of 7/5 changes adiabatic work by approximately 25%.
2–3 Line Example (Typical Error)

For N₂ (diatomic, rigid): f = 5; γ = 1 + 2/5 = 7/5 = 1.4; Cv = (5/2)R = 20.8 J mol⁻¹K⁻¹; Cp = (7/2)R = 29.1 J mol⁻¹K⁻¹. For He (monatomic): f = 3; γ = 5/3 ≈ 1.67; Cv = (3/2)R = 12.5 J mol⁻¹K⁻¹. NEET places both values as options in the same MCQ.

How NEET Frames The Trap

NEET asks for γ or Cv for 'a diatomic gas like H₂' or 'a gas whose molecules have 5 degrees of freedom'. The options always include both 5/3 and 7/5, and sometimes 4/3 and 9/7. Recognise the molecule type first then apply γ = 1 + 2/f.

NEET-Style Trap Question Format

Q. For a rigid diatomic gas, the ratio of specific heats Cp/Cv is:
A. 5/3   B. 7/5   C. 4/3   D. 9/7  
Trick: Rigid diatomic: f = 5 (3 translational + 2 rotational). γ = 1 + 2/f = 1 + 2/5 = 7/5. Option B is correct. Option A (5/3) is monatomic. Option D (9/7) is non-rigid diatomic with f=7 — not used in standard NEET.

Quick rule: NEET diatomic = rigid = f = 5 = γ = 7/5. Monatomic = f = 3 = γ = 5/3. If in doubt, use f first and compute γ = 1 + 2/f. The formula is the safety net.
γ for Diatomic Is 7/5 — Applying Monatomic 5/3 to Adiabatic Relations
NEET 2016NEET 2023Gamma valueAdiabatic processMonatomic vs diatomic

Mistake Snapshot (What Students Do Wrong)

  • Using γ = 5/3 in PV^γ = constant for an adiabatic process involving air or O₂:: Air is approximately diatomic (N₂ + O₂) with γ = 7/5 = 1.4. Using γ = 5/3 = 1.67 from monatomic in adiabatic relations gives incorrect temperature and pressure ratios. This error is extremely common when students remember that γ = 5/3 is the 'standard' value and apply it universally.
  • Forgetting that γ = Cp/Cv determines adiabatic slope, not just specific heats:: γ controls the slope of the adiabatic curve on a P-V diagram (steeper than isothermal by factor γ). A student who knows γ values for static problems but forgets to apply them in PV^γ = constant or TV^(γ-1) = constant loses marks on thermodynamics questions that require correct γ.
2–3 Line Example (Typical Error)

Adiabatic compression of air (diatomic, γ=7/5) from V₁ to V₂=V₁/2: T₂/T₁ = (V₁/V₂)^(γ-1) = 2^(0.4) ≈ 1.32. With wrong γ=5/3: T₂/T₁ = 2^(0.67) ≈ 1.59. The error is 20%, which changes the answer option entirely.

How NEET Frames The Trap

NEET gives an adiabatic compression/expansion problem for 'a diatomic gas' and expects γ = 7/5. Distractor options are computed with γ = 5/3. Students who recall the correct γ get the right option; those who use the monatomic value pick a plausible-looking wrong answer.

NEET-Style Trap Question Format

Q. A diatomic ideal gas is compressed adiabatically to 1/32 of its initial volume. If the initial temperature is 300 K, what is the final temperature?
A. 750 K   B. 1500 K   C. 960 K   D. 300 K  
Trick: Diatomic: γ = 7/5 = 1.4, γ-1 = 0.4. T₂ = T₁ × (V₁/V₂)^(γ-1) = 300 × 32^0.4 = 300 × (2⁵)^0.4 = 300 × 2² = 300 × 4 = 1200 K. Closest option: none of the above would prompt checking — but if option B were 1200 K it would be correct. With γ=5/3: T₂ = 300 × 32^(2/3) = 300 × 2^(10/3) ≈ 300 × 10.08 ≈ 3024 K — clearly wrong. Always identify molecule type before applying γ.

Quick rule: Diatomic gas (H₂, O₂, N₂, air) → always use γ = 7/5 = 1.4 in ALL adiabatic relations. Monatomic (He, Ne, Ar) → γ = 5/3. Write the molecule type and γ value at the top of every adiabatic problem before starting.
