Chapter Snapshot - Gravitation

One of the most formula-rich chapters in NEET physics, Gravitation demands mastery of three interlinked pillars: Newton's law of gravitation and variation of g, gravitational potential energy and escape velocity, and orbital mechanics with Kepler's laws. The chapter sits at the boundary of classical mechanics and celestial physics — every major formula involves G or g, and the majority of NEET traps stem from sign errors in potential energy (U = −GMm/r is negative) or confusing the direction of change of orbital speed versus time period with altitude. Students who commit the five key expressions (g, g′ at height, g′ at depth, v_escape, v_orbital) and the three Kepler laws to instant recall will reliably score 3 marks here.

Subtopics - Gravitation (NEET)

Four major blocks: Newton's law of gravitation and variation of g with altitude, depth, latitude, and Earth's rotation; gravitational potential and potential energy including escape velocity; orbital motion, satellite mechanics, and geostationary orbits; and Kepler's three laws of planetary motion with applications.

Revision tip: Before any Gravitation numerical, classify the problem: (1) Is it about g variation → identify altitude vs depth and use correct formula. (2) Is it about energy → remember U = −GMm/r is negative and total energy E = −GMm/2r for circular orbit. (3) Is it about orbital motion → v_o = √(GM/r), T² ∝ r³. (4) Is it about Kepler → identify which law and whether angular momentum conservation is needed. This four-step triage eliminates formula confusion.

1) Newton's Law of Gravitation and Variation of g

Establishes the universal law F = Gm₁m₂/r² (G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² kg⁻²) and derives surface gravity g = GM/R² = 9.8 m/s². Then derives four variation formulae: with altitude g′ = g/(1+h/R)² ≈ g(1−2h/R) for h<<R; with depth g′ = g(1−d/R), linear decrease, g=0 at centre; with latitude g′ = g − Rω²cos²λ, poles > equator; effect of Earth's rotation reducing apparent weight. Key inequality: g is maximum at the surface.

Universal Law of Gravitation and GF = Gm₁m₂/r² where G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² kg⁻². G is universal constant — does not vary with medium, temperature, or position. Force is along the line joining the two masses, attractive in nature, central force, obeys principle of superposition. g = GM/R² at Earth's surface. Relationship between G and g: g = GM/R², so M = gR²/G. g on Moon ≈ g/6 because while M_moon is 80 times less, R_moon is 4 times less, net g_moon ≈ 1.62 m/s². Weight W = mg varies with location; mass is invariant.
›
Variation of g with Altitude and DepthAbove surface (altitude h): g′ = GM/(R+h)² = g × R²/(R+h)². For h << R: g′ ≈ g(1 − 2h/R). Rate of decrease = 2g/R per unit height — decreases faster with height than with depth for same shift. Below surface (depth d): g′ = g(1 − d/R). Linear decrease; g = 0 at centre (d=R). Maximum g is at Earth's surface, not at centre or above. For same fractional change: 2% decrease in g requires h = R/100 above surface but d = R/100 below surface halves the fractional decrease — height formula gives twice the change of depth formula for same distance.
›
Variation of g with Latitude and Earth's RotationDue to Earth's rotation: g′ = g − Rω²cos²λ where λ is latitude. At equator (λ=0): max reduction, g′ = g − Rω². At poles (λ=90°): no reduction, g_pole = g. Hence g_pole > g_equator. Two combined factors: (1) Earth is oblate — poles are closer to centre so g_pole > g_equator due to smaller radius. (2) Centrifugal pseudo-force at equator further reduces apparent g. Combined: g_pole − g_equator ≈ 0.052 m/s²≈ 0.5% difference. Rockets launched eastward at equator to exploit Earth's rotational velocity ≈ 465 m/s as free boost.

2) Gravitational Potential Energy and Escape Velocity

Defines gravitational potential V = −GM/r (scalar, always negative) and gravitational PE U = −GMm/r (always negative, zero at infinity). Establishes that more negative PE means more tightly bound. Derives escape velocity v_e = √(2GM/R) = √(2gR) ≈ 11.2 km/s — independent of mass of projectile. Shows binding energy = GMm/(2r) = ½mv_e² at surface. Key property: total mechanical energy E = KE + PE = −GMm/(2r) for circular orbit (negative = bound).

U = −GMm/rv_escape = √(2gR) ≈ 11.2 km/sIndependent of projectile massBinding energy = GMm/2r
›
Gravitational Potential and Potential EnergyGravitational potential V = −GM/r (J/kg); always negative; V=0 at r=∞. Gravitational PE of mass m at distance r: U = mV = −GMm/r (J); always negative. At Earth's surface: U_surface = −GMm/R. Change in PE to move mass from surface to height h: ΔU = GMm(1/R − 1/(R+h)) = mgh/(1+h/R) ≈ mgh for h<<R. PE of system of 4 particles at corners of square of side a each of mass m: U = −4Gm²/a − 2Gm²(a√2) = −Gm²/a(4 + √2). Inside a uniform shell: gravitational field = 0, so potential = constant = −GM/R throughout interior. Sign reminder: U more negative = lower potential = more bound = harder to escape.
›
Escape Velocity and Binding EnergyEscape velocity v_e: minimum speed for object to escape gravitational field from surface. Energy condition: KE = −PE → ½mv_e² = GMm/R → v_e = √(2GM/R) = √(2gR). At Earth's surface: v_e = √(2 × 9.8 × 6.4×10⁶) ≈ 11.2 km/s. CRITICAL: v_e is independent of mass of projectile (m cancels). Does not depend on direction of projection. Relationship with orbital speed at surface: v_e = √2 × v_orbital. Escape from height h: v_e(h) = √(2GM/(R+h)) = v_e × √(R/(R+h)) — decreases with height. Binding energy = energy required to move to infinity = GMm/(2R) at surface for satellite in circular orbit. From infinity falling to surface: body gains speed = v_e.

3) Orbital Motion and Satellites

Derives orbital velocity v_o = √(GM/r) = √(gR²/r) and shows it DECREASES as height increases. Derives time period T = 2π√(r³/GM) and shows it INCREASES as height increases. For surface orbit: v_o ≈ 7.9 km/s, T ≈ 84 min. Geostationary satellite: T = 24 h, h ≈ 36,000 km, equatorial orbit, west-to-east. Total energy of satellite in circular orbit: E = −GMm/(2r); KE = GMm/(2r); PE = −GMm/r; |PE| = 2 × KE always. Binding energy = GMm/(2r). Weightlessness in orbit is due to free fall, not absence of gravity.

v_o = √(GM/r) decreases with rT = 2π√(r³/GM) increases with rGeostationary: T=24h, h=36000kmE_total = −GMm/2r
›
Orbital Velocity and Time PeriodOrbital velocity: gravitational force provides centripetal force → GMm/r² = mv²/r → v_o = √(GM/r). Rewriting using g = GM/R²: v_o = R√(g/r) = √(gR²/r). At surface (r=R): v_o = √(gR) ≈ 7.9 km/s. As r increases: v_o DECREASES (inverse √r). At height h: v_o = √(gR²/(R+h)). Time period: T = 2πr/v_o = 2π√(r³/GM). As r increases: T INCREASES (proportional to r^(3/2)). Near earth surface: T ≈ 2π√(R³/GM) = 2π√(R/g) ≈ 84 min = 1 h 24 min. Geostationary orbit: T = 24 h, orbit radius r ≈ 42,250 km, height h ≈ 35,880 km ≈ 36,000 km. Geostationary characteristics: equatorial orbit, west-to-east direction, appears stationary from Earth, used for communications (INSAT group).
›
Energy of Satellite and WeightlessnessFor circular orbit of radius r: KE = ½mv² = GMm/(2r); PE = −GMm/r; Total E = KE + PE = −GMm/(2r) (negative = bound). Therefore |PE| = 2 × KE (virial theorem for gravity). Binding energy = −E_total = GMm/(2r). To move from orbit r₁ to r₂ (r₂>r₁): additional KE needed = ½GMm(1/r₁ − 1/r₂). If orbit radius decreases by 1%: speed increases by 0.5% (v ∝ 1/√r). Weightlessness: g inside satellite ≠ 0; astronaut is in constant free fall — normal force from floor = 0. Objects in satellite are in same free-fall orbit, so relative acceleration = 0. Rockets launched eastward at equator: Earth's rotational speed ≈ 465 m/s adds to launch speed.

4) Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

States and applies all three Kepler laws: (1) Law of orbits — planets move in ellipses with Sun at one focus; (2) Law of areas — radius vector sweeps equal areas in equal times (dA/dt = constant = L/2m, consequence of angular momentum conservation); (3) Law of periods — T² ∝ a³ where a is semi-major axis, equivalently T² ∝ r³ for circular orbit. For circular orbit: T²/r³ = 4π²/GM = constant for all planets around same star. From Kepler 2: planet is fastest at perihelion (closest) and slowest at aphelion (farthest). Kinetic energy is highest at perihelion, lowest at aphelion.

Elliptical orbitdA/dt = L/2m = constantT² ∝ a³Fastest at perihelion
›
Kepler's First and Second LawsFirst law (Law of Orbits): Every planet revolves around the Sun in an elliptical orbit with the Sun at one of the two foci of the ellipse. Circle is a special case of ellipse. Second law (Law of Areas): The line joining a planet to the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal intervals of time. Consequence of angular momentum conservation: L = mvr = constant for central force. Since dA/dt = L/(2m) = constant, and L = mvr: when r is small (perihelion, closest point), v must be large to keep L constant → planet moves fastest. When r is large (aphelion, farthest point), v is small → planet moves slowest. At perihelion: KE maximum, PE minimum (U more negative), total E constant. At aphelion: KE minimum, PE maximum (U less negative). For ellipse SB perpendicular to major axis: K_C > K_B > K_A where C is perihelion, A is aphelion.
›
Kepler's Third Law and ApplicationsThird law (Law of Periods): The square of the time period of revolution of a planet is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis of its elliptical orbit. T² ∝ a³, or T² = (4π²/GM) × r³ for circular orbit. T₁²/T₂² = r₁³/r₂³ for any two planets around the same Sun. Application: ratio of distances d₁/d₂ known → T₁/T₂ = (d₁/d₂)^(3/2). Example: if d₁/d₂ = 1.38, then T₁/T₂ = 1.38^(3/2). Frequency n = 1/T, so n₁²d₁³ = n₂²d₂³ = constant (Kepler 3rd in frequency form). Geostationary orbit derived from T² ∝ r³: known T_geo = 24 h, T_nearearth ≈ 1.4 h → r_geo/R = (24/1.4)^(2/3) ≈ 6.6 × R. Kepler 3rd also validates Newton's inverse-square law: combining v_o = √(GM/r) and T = 2πr/v gives T² = 4π²r³/GM ∝ r³.

Mistake Snapshot (What Students Do Wrong)

  • Using altitude approximation g(1−2h/R) for a depth problem:: The formula g′ = g(1−2h/R) is valid ONLY for small heights above the surface. For depth d below the surface, the correct formula is g′ = g(1−d/R) — linear decrease with NO factor of 2. Using the altitude formula for a depth question overestimates the reduction of g by a factor of 2 and gives a wrong answer. NEET always provides the wrong altitude-formula answer as a distractor for depth problems.
  • Claiming g is maximum at the centre of Earth or in outer space:: g is maximum at Earth's surface. Below the surface g decreases linearly to zero at the centre. Above the surface g decreases as inverse-square. At the centre g = 0, not maximum. NEET assertion-reason questions directly test this: 'A body becomes weightless at the centre of Earth' is a TRUE assertion with the reason that g=0 there — NOT that gravity does not exist.
2–3 Line Example (Typical Error)

Body at depth d = R/2 below surface. Correct: g′ = g(1 − 1/2) = g/2. Using wrong altitude formula: g′ ≈ g(1 − 2×(R/2)/R) = g(1−1) = 0 — gives zero, which is the answer for the centre only. NEET provides g/2 and 0 as options; altitude-formula error leads to the wrong option 0.

How NEET Frames The Trap

NEET asks: 'A body is taken to a depth R/2 below Earth's surface. What is the new value of g?' and provides options g/2, 0, g/4, 2g. Students who memorise 'altitude formula' without reading 'depth' select 0 — wrong.

NEET-Style Trap Question Format

Q. The radius of Earth is R. At what depth below the surface is the acceleration due to gravity equal to half its surface value?
A. R/2   B. R/4   C. R/3   D. 2R/3  
Trick: Depth formula: g′ = g(1−d/R) = g/2 → 1−d/R = 1/2 → d = R/2. Option A correct. Using altitude formula g(1−2h/R) = g/2 → h = R/4 — this gives Option B, which is the distractor for students who confuse altitude and depth formulas.

Quick rule: Formula has factor 2 → ALTITUDE. Formula has no factor 2 → DEPTH. g′ = g(1−2h/R) altitude; g′ = g(1−d/R) depth. Maximum g at surface; g=0 at centre.
Escape Velocity Is Independent of Projectile Mass
NEET 2018NEET 2021Escape velocityMass independenceConceptual trap

Mistake Snapshot (What Students Do Wrong)

  • Claiming escape velocity depends on mass m of the body being projected:: From energy conservation: ½mv_e² = GMm/R → v_e = √(2GM/R). The mass m cancels completely. Escape velocity is the same for a hydrogen atom and a spacecraft launched from the same surface. NEET provides planet escape velocity questions where students factor in body mass and get an answer that includes m — this is always wrong.
  • Confusing escape velocity with orbital velocity and using v_e = v_o instead of v_e = √2 v_o:: At the same radius r: v_e = √(2GM/r) and v_o = √(GM/r), so v_e = √2 × v_o ≈ 1.414 × v_o. A satellite in orbit needs √2 times its orbital speed to escape — an additional factor of √2, not a factor of 2.
2–3 Line Example (Typical Error)

Escape velocity on a planet where radius = 4R_E and escape velocity = 3v_e_E. What is its mass? v_e_planet² = 2GM_planet/(4R) = 9v_e_E² = 9 × 2gR → GM_planet = 9 × 2gR × 2R = 36gR² → M_planet = 36gR²/G = 36M_E (since gR² = GM_E). Correct: mass = 36M_E. NEET provides options including values divided by m — always wrong.

How NEET Frames The Trap

NEET states: 'Escape velocity of a body of 1 kg mass on a planet is 100 m/s. Gravitational potential energy of the body at that planet is?' Gravitational PE = −GMm/R = −½mv_e² = −½×1×10000 = −5000 J. The mass DOES appear here in PE — trap is misapplying where m appears (in PE, not in v_e).

NEET-Style Trap Question Format

Q. The escape velocity from Earth is 11.2 km/s. What will be the escape velocity from a planet having twice the radius and the same mean density as Earth?
A. 22.4 km/s   B. 11.2 km/s   C. 5.6 km/s   D. 15.8 km/s  
Trick: v_e = √(2GM/R) = √(2G × (4/3πR³ρ)/R) = R√(8πGρ/3). So v_e ∝ R when density is constant. Planet has R_p = 2R_E → v_e_planet = 2 × 11.2 = 22.4 km/s. Option A correct. Common mistake: not recognising v_e ∝ R with same density, instead using M unchanged and getting wrong answer.

Quick rule: Escape velocity: m cancels → INDEPENDENT of projectile mass. v_e = √(2gR) at surface. v_e = √2 × v_orbital at same radius. v_e ∝ R when density is constant (M ∝ R³ then v_e ∝ R).
Orbital Velocity Decreases with Height — Time Period Increases
NEET 2019NEET 2023Orbital velocitySatellite time periodDirection confusion

Mistake Snapshot (What Students Do Wrong)

  • Claiming orbital velocity increases as satellite height increases:: v_o = √(GM/r). As r increases, v_o decreases — inversely proportional to √r. This is counterintuitive because a higher orbit seems to require 'more effort and speed'. In reality, the gravitational pull is weaker at higher orbits, so less centripetal force is needed, achieved by a slower speed. A 1% decrease in orbital radius causes a 0.5% increase in speed.
  • Claiming time period decreases when satellite moves to a higher orbit:: T = 2π√(r³/GM) ∝ r^(3/2). As r increases, T increases. Higher orbit = slower speed AND larger circumference → much longer period. Geostationary orbit at 36,000 km has T = 24 h. Near-Earth orbit at ~400 km has T ≈ 90 min. The period increases dramatically with altitude.
2–3 Line Example (Typical Error)

Satellite A at orbital radius R, satellite B at 4R. Speed ratio: v_A/v_B = √(r_B/r_A) = √(4R/R) = 2. Period ratio: T_A/T_B = (r_A/r_B)^(3/2) = (1/4)^(3/2) = 1/8. So T_B = 8T_A. B at higher orbit is SLOWER (half the speed) and has period 8 times longer. Students who invert the speed ratio get T ratio inverted too — double error.

How NEET Frames The Trap

NEET: 'A satellite is moving around Earth with speed V in a circular orbit of radius r. If orbit radius is decreased by 1%, its speed will...' Answer: increase by 0.5% (v ∝ r^{−1/2}; −1/2 × (−1%) = +0.5%). Trap options include 'decrease by 0.5%' (wrong sign).

NEET-Style Trap Question Format

Q. Two satellites of same mass orbit Earth at heights h₁ and h₂ where h₂ > h₁. Which of the following is correct?
A. Orbital speed: v₁ > v₂ and Time period: T₁ > T₂   B. Orbital speed: v₁ < v₂ and Time period: T₁ < T₂   C. Orbital speed: v₁ > v₂ and Time period: T₁ < T₂   D. Orbital speed: v₁ < v₂ and Time period: T₁ > T₂  
Trick: h₂ > h₁ → r₂ > r₁. v_o ∝ 1/√r → v₁ > v₂ (satellite at lower height is faster). T ∝ r^(3/2) → T₁ < T₂ (lower orbit has shorter period). Option C is correct: v₁ > v₂ AND T₁ < T₂. Both change in the 'same direction' — lower orbit → faster speed AND shorter period. Students who confuse one direction get option A or D.

Quick rule: Higher orbit → LOWER orbital speed (v ∝ 1/√r). Higher orbit → LONGER period (T ∝ r^(3/2)). Both trends go the SAME way: lower orbit = faster speed AND shorter period. Remember: GPS satellites at 20,000 km orbit slower than ISS at 400 km.
g at Earth's Centre Is Zero — Maximum g Is at Surface
NEET 2017NEET 2022g at centreAssertion-reasonConceptual trap

Mistake Snapshot (What Students Do Wrong)

  • Claiming g is maximum at Earth's centre because everything is 'pulled from all sides':: g = 0 at the centre, NOT maximum. Using depth formula: g′ = g(1−d/R); at d=R (centre): g′ = g(1−1) = 0. The shell theorem explains this: at the centre, all gravitational contributions from surrounding mass cancel by symmetry. Maximum g is at the surface (d=0 from the surface).
  • Claiming weightlessness at centre implies no gravitational mass nearby:: The Earth's entire mass surrounds you at the centre but the net gravitational field is zero due to symmetrical cancellation. Weight W = mg′ = 0 at centre because g′=0, not because mass is absent. NEET assertion-reason: 'A body becomes weightless at the centre — Reason: As distance from centre decreases, g increases' — WRONG reason. Correct reason: g at centre = 0 due to symmetric cancellation.
2–3 Line Example (Typical Error)

Body of weight 500 N on Earth surface. Weight at depth d=R/2: g′ = g(1−1/2) = g/2; W′ = 250 N. Weight at depth d=R (centre): g′ = 0; W′ = 0. Variation: surface=max, decreasing below surface, zero at centre, also decreasing above surface as 1/r². NEET MCQ: weight vs depth graph should show linear decrease from surface to centre — zero at centre, not a maximum.

How NEET Frames The Trap

NEET assertion — 'A body becomes weightless at the centre of Earth. Reason: As the distance from the centre of earth decreases, acceleration due to gravity increases.' Assertion is TRUE. Reason is FALSE (g decreases with depth, not increases). Trap: student selects A (both true and reason explains assertion) instead of C (assertion true, reason false).

NEET-Style Trap Question Format

Q. At which of the following locations is the acceleration due to gravity maximum?
A. At the centre of Earth   B. At the equator   C. At the poles   D. At height R above Earth's surface  
Trick: g is maximum at the surface. At poles g is slightly larger than at equator because Earth is oblate (poles are closer to centre) and no centrifugal reduction. Option C (at poles) is the maximum among these choices. Option A (centre) has g=0. Option D (height R) has g=g/4. Between equator and poles, poles win. Correct: C.

Quick rule: g profile: increases from centre (g=0) to surface (g=max), then decreases above surface as 1/r². Maximum g is at the Earth's SURFACE. g=0 ONLY at the centre (by symmetry). Weightlessness at centre: g=0, not 'infinite symmetrical pull'.
